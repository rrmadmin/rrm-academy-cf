# Commentary Content Strategy: "The Bridge"

**Date:** 2026-03-25
**Status:** Draft spec

## Core Premise

The commentary blog is the bridge between the 3,200-article research library and the patient's next appointment. Every post connects three things:

1. **A study from the library** (the evidence)
2. **A patient experience the reader recognizes** (the emotional hook)
3. **Something the reader can do, say, or ask** (the action)

Formula: Doctor mind + patient experience + research = actionable advice.

## Primary Reader

A woman mid-journey. She has already found RRM, is charting, or is actively exploring restorative care. She wants to go deeper, understand the evidence, and feel confident in her choice. She is not discovering RRM for the first time.

She reads commentary posts to feel equipped, not educated. The pillar guides and courses educate. The blog equips.

## Voice

Naomi's clinical perspective delivered with passion and warmth. Not angry at the system. Not lecturing. She is the doctor who gets it, translating research into what it means for the reader's life. She names what the reader has experienced, shows her the evidence, and tells her what to do with it.

Key qualities:
- Opens with the patient moment, not the study
- Uses specific numbers (never vague "research suggests")
- Connects data to the reader's chart, appointment, or conversation
- Closes with action, not summary
- Couple-centered language in fertility contexts

## Five Post Types

Every post maps to one of five patient-value categories. Each category answers a different emotional need.

### 1. "You were right to question that"

**Reader walks away with:** Confidence that her instinct was correct, backed by data.

**Pattern:** She questioned something (a diagnosis, a recommendation, a dismissal). This study proves her instinct was sound. She now has the evidence to trust herself.

**Example:** Study shows 90.7% of "unexplained infertility" patients had undiagnosed endometriosis at laparoscopy. "Your infertility was never unexplained. It was undiagnosed."

### 2. "Here's what that means for you"

**Reader walks away with:** Understanding of what a study's findings mean for her body, her chart, her situation.

**Pattern:** New research that translates directly into something she can observe or understand about her own health. Naomi explains it like she would across the desk.

**Example:** 55% of healthy women had at least one short luteal phase over 12 months. "Your cycle is telling you something, but only if you're tracking it with precision."

### 3. "Bring this to your next appointment"

**Reader walks away with:** A specific question, request, or data point she can use with her provider.

**Pattern:** Evidence that changes what she should ask for or expect from her care. Ends with concrete questions or requests she can bring to a clinical encounter.

**Example:** Inositol meta-analysis shows non-inferiority to metformin for PCOS. "Three things to ask your doctor before accepting metformin as your only option."

### 4. "This is what good care looks like"

**Reader walks away with:** Ability to evaluate whether she is getting thorough, restorative care.

**Pattern:** A study that demonstrates what a complete workup, proper surgical approach, or thorough treatment protocol looks like. She can compare it to what she is receiving.

**Example:** OPTIMUM protocol for RPL: test for thyroid, immune, endometritis, thrombophilia. Live birth rate from 42% to 78%. "Here's the difference between 'just try again' and a real investigation."

### 5. "You're not doing this alone"

**Reader walks away with:** Connection, normalization, reassurance grounded in data.

**Pattern:** A study that validates her experience as common, not unusual. She is not the only one. Data becomes comfort.

**Example:** 41% of women trying to conceive can't identify their fertile window. "You're ahead of where most people start."

## Post Structure

Every post follows a consistent rhythm:

1. **Open with the patient moment** (2-3 sentences). The experience she recognizes. No study citation yet. Meet her where she is emotionally.
2. **Introduce the evidence** (1-2 paragraphs). The study, the numbers, what researchers found. Specific data points, sample size, journal.
3. **Naomi translates** (2-3 paragraphs). What this means clinically. Why it matters for her specifically. Connect to charting, RRM approach, what she is already doing or should be doing.
4. **The actionable close** (bulleted or short paragraph). What she can do, ask, bring to an appointment, or look for in her own chart. Concrete. Not vague.
5. **Link to the library article** and 1-2 related resources on the site (pillar guide, FAQ, course).

**Word count:** 600-900 words. Not long-form education. Focused, one-study, one-takeaway posts.

## Production Model

### Cadence
- 8 posts per month
- Never more than 5 days between posts
- Publish on consistent days (e.g., Mon/Thu) for reader expectation

### Pipeline
1. **Article selection** (Brian + Claude): Pick 8 library articles mapped to post types and domain gaps
2. **Gianna drafts** (batch): All 8 drafted in one session, each linked to its library article
3. **Naomi batch review** (2x/month): 4 posts per review session. ~2 hours per session.
4. **Review depth by post type:**
   - Light review: Types 1, 2, 5 (research translation, data-grounded, lower clinical risk)
   - Full review: Types 3, 4 (clinical recommendations, actionable medical guidance)
5. **Publish**: Through existing Airtable editorial pipeline

### Gianna Production Rules
- Each commentary anchors on one primary library article but weaves in NaPro textbook chapters and collaborator network references
- All statistics must trace to the article's abstract, full text, or a cited textbook chapter (PG-G7)
- The 14 Gianna proof gates apply to every draft
- Gianna reads the full text from R2 when available (deep tier)
- Each post must include the library article link for the reader

### Article Selection Methodology
Layer 1: Naomi's own publications and presentations first
Layer 2: Collaborator network (Yeung, Hilgers, Stanford, Boyle, Sinervo, Redwine)
Layer 3: Top RRM tradition authors and domain evidence
Layer 4: Broader library only to fill remaining gaps

For each article, rrm-cli traces the full citation chain: primary article, relevant NaPro textbook chapters (with their reference lists), and graph traversal for connected articles. Raw D1 queries are a fallback, not the starting point.

## Batch 1: First 8 Posts

Each post anchors on a primary article and is backed by NaPro textbook chapters and the RRM collaborator network.

### Slot 1: NaPro Outcomes -- "Here's what that means for you"
- **Primary:** Sanchez-Mendez et al., 2025. 1,310-couple NaPro cohort. PMID 41323405.
- **Headline:** 62.1% adjusted THBR; 2.5 diagnoses per "unexplained" couple; 83.7% at age 18-30
- **Textbook:** Ch. 40 (NaPro in Infertility), Ch. 51 (Effectiveness -- fecundability rates NaPro vs IVF, 1,045 patients), Ch. 38 (Trends and Deficiencies)
- **Network:** Stanford/Boyle 2008 (52.8% at 24 months, PMID 18772291), Tham 2012 (66% adjusted, Canadian), de Groot 2018 (32.1% post-IVF)
- **Layer:** 2 (Collaborator network)

### Slot 2: Body Literacy -- "You're not doing this alone"
- **Primary:** Wainwright et al., 2025. 97,414 women TTC. PMID 40846945.
- **Headline:** 41% can't ID fertile window; irregular cycles 3x odds of not knowing; CrMS identifies ovulation day with 94.5% accuracy
- **Textbook:** Ch. 61 (Preconceptional Care -- 94.5% Peak Day accuracy in 432 cycles), Ch. 14 (Objective Mucus Classification -- validated scoring system), Ch. 35 (Luteal Phase Deficiencies)
- **Network:** Stanford 2015 (Fertil Steril -- cervical fluid identifies 2-3 days pre-ovulation), Fehring FABM research, Colombo 2000 (7,017 cycles fecundability)
- **Layer:** 3 (Domain)

### Slot 3: NCL/S-MAP -- "This is what good care looks like"
- **Primary:** Whittaker NM, AAGL 2024. 4 rare cancers via S-MAP + NCL. Conference presentation.
- **Headline:** 89.1% of "normal" laparoscopies had missed endometriosis (Ch. 63); 4 cancers found with probability 0.0000000001
- **Textbook:** Ch. 63 (Diagnostic Laparoscopy: Near Contact -- the 46-patient repeat lap study), Ch. 65 (Atlas of Findings), Ch. 70-73 (PEARS series)
- **Ch. 63 references:** Goldstein 1980 (adolescent endo), Redwine 1987 (age distribution, Fertil Steril), Martin/Redwine 1990 (Color Atlas), Hilgers 1992 (Veress needle technique), Batt/Wheeler 1992 (Advanced diagnostic laparoscopy)
- **Source material:** Full case series text with 4 patient narratives (Case D: told to get hysterectomy, got NCL/S-MAP instead, found cancer, treated it, brought home a baby)
- **Network:** Redwine PMID 3190209 (NCL origin, 75% complete relief in 400 women), Yeung 2024 repeat surgery rate, Whittaker-Yeung 2025 joint piece
- **Layer:** 1 (Naomi's own work)

### Slot 4: PCOS -- "Bring this to your next appointment"
- **Primary:** Combined approach: myo-inositol evidence + Hilgers Ch. 75 (PEARS: Ovarian Wedge Resection)
- **Headline:** OWR: 91% ovulation resumption, 92% pregnancy rate (Halbe, Ch. 75); Inositol: 86% ovulation (Nestler 1999 NEJM), 70% restored in 3,602 patients (Regidor 2016)
- **Textbook:** Ch. 75 (PEARS for PCOS -- OWR removing 1/2-3/4 tissue, near-zero adhesions vs drilling's 20-70%), Ch. 41 (HPO Dysfunction), Ch. 45 (Amenorrhea/Anovulation), Ch. 46 (Medical Treatment -- low-dose clomiphene, 3.2% multiple pregnancy rate)
- **Ch. 75 references:** Stein/Leventhal 1935 (seminal), Stein 1965 (95% cycle resumption, 85% conception in 108 cases), Halbe 1972 (91%/92%), Adashi 1981 (90 consecutive cases), adhesion studies (Dabirashrafi 1991, Gurgan 1991, Naether 1993)
- **Addendum:** Letrozole supersedes clomiphene as first-line per 2023 guidelines
- **Network:** Nestler NEJM 1999 (PMID 10219066, D-chiro-inositol), Greff 2023 meta-analysis (26 RCTs, non-inferior to metformin), Yildirim 2003 (134 patients, 90% pregnancy via minilaparotomy)
- **Layer:** 3 (Domain + Textbook)

### Slot 5: Male Factor -- "You were right to question that"
- **Primary:** Grande et al., 2025. 1,014 couples, multicentric. PMID 39930939.
- **Headline:** Idiopathic dropped from 30-50% to 8% with comprehensive workup; 40.9% natural pregnancy; 48.8% had genital tract inflammation
- **Textbook:** Ch. 47 (Male Infertility -- 538 patients, 75% clomiphene response in oligospermia at 5 mg BID, lowest ESC achieving pregnancy 30,000/cc, Kruger criteria critique)
- **Ch. 47 references:** Sigman/Lipshultz 1997, Kruger 1988 (IVF-specific criteria misapplied to natural conception), Ronnberg 1980 (clomiphene response), Lenzi 1993 (glutathione RCT)
- **Network:** Esteves SDF guidelines 2020 (PMID 33108829), Cochrane antioxidants (4.85x live birth improvement), Stanford 2021 (4.9 diagnoses/couple in RRM primary care)
- **Layer:** 3 (Domain)

### Slot 6: RPL/Miscarriage -- "You're not doing this alone"
- **Primary:** Boyle et al., 2024. DHEA for hypoandrogenemia, 5 recurrent miscarriages. PMID 38426161.
- **Headline:** After 5 losses with no diagnosis from standard RPL workup, RRM identified hypoandrogenemia. DHEA restoration produced a live birth at 36 weeks. Ch. 57: 79% successful pregnancy in women with 3+ prior losses. Ch. 50: 84% success for women <=37.
- **Textbook:** Ch. 57 (Recurrent SAB -- 79% success with NaPro protocol, HCG support protocol, thyroid/T3 data, 47 references), Ch. 58 (Early Pregnancy Loss -- Wilcox 22-25% early loss rate, Pope Paul VI Institute 1,704 pregnancies), Ch. 54 (Progesterone Assessment -- 610 patients/8,545 levels), Ch. 55 (Progesterone Support -- safety data across 933 patients, no anomaly increase)
- **Network:** Boyle 2022 (16 years infertility + 3 RPL + 8 failed IVF, PMID 35729591), Carpentier/Stanford/Boyle NEJM 2016 (PROMISE trial critique), Coomarasamy PRISM 2019 (NNT=8 for 3+ losses), Daya 1988 (40% LPD in recurrent aborters, 81% success with progesterone)
- **Layer:** 2 (Collaborator network)

### Slot 7: Endo Outcomes -- "Here's what that means for you"
- **Primary:** Yeung et al., 2024. Repeat surgery rate after optimal excision. Preprint.
- **Headline:** 2.5% repeat surgery rate (15/620) vs 98.7% recurrence with traditional cauterization (Ch. 79)
- **Textbook:** Ch. 79 (Recurrence after PEARS -- excision 7.2% site recurrence vs vaporization 22.3%, p=.0044; 81.8% of recurrences were NEW areas, not persistence), Ch. 42 (Endo and Fertility -- 77.6% limited/dry mucus, 54% luteal defects, 60.3% abnormal ovulation), Ch. 70 (PEARS technique), Ch. 62 (What is Surgical NaPro)
- **Network:** Yeung 2011 (teenagers, excision without suppression, PMID 21316047), Yeung 2013 (multicenter feasibility, PMID 24018069), Yeung 2016 (deep retraction pockets -- 30% of clear DRPs had endo on histology), Whittaker-Yeung 2025 joint piece, Redwine (cited throughout Ch. 42 with 1,785-patient anatomic distribution data)
- **Layer:** 2 (Collaborator network)

### Slot 8: Cycle Science -- "Here's what that means for you"
- **Primary:** Ng et al., 2020. Endometrial decidualization review. PMID 32521725. (Open access)
- **Secondary anchor:** Henry/Prior et al., 2024. Luteal phase variability in healthy women. PMID 39320898.
- **Headline:** 75% of pregnancy losses trace to implantation failure; 55% of healthy women had at least one short luteal phase; day-21 progesterone is fundamentally flawed
- **Textbook:** Ch. 19 (Targeted Hormone Assessment -- 92.7% periovulatory targeting accuracy, 96.3% postovulatory; day-21 misdiagnosis case study; endo patients significantly decreased progesterone at mid-post-Peak), Ch. 35 (Luteal Phase Deficiencies -- Type I/II/III classification), Ch. 27 (Cooperative P+E2 Replacement), Ch. 24 (Normal Hormone Levels)
- **Network:** Prior 1982-2025 (subclinical ovulatory disturbance body of work -- marathon training, vegetarian diet, spinal bone loss), Miller 2015 (36% LPD in infertile couples), Balasch 1986 (86.3% of LPD patients had NORMAL hormone levels)
- **Note:** Ch. 24, 27, 35 need body text ingestion (only metadata currently in knowledge base)
- **Layer:** 4 (Graph traversal from Boyle DHEA)
