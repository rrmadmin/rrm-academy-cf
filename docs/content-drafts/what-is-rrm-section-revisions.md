<h2 id="rrm-vs-ivf">RRM vs. IVF: A Detailed Comparison</h2>

<p>People search "RRM vs. IVF" because they want to understand how these approaches differ. This section exists to meet that question directly. But the framing matters. RRM is its own paradigm. It is not a stepping stone to IVF, a consolation prize for those who cannot afford IVF, or a religious alternative to IVF. It is a medical discipline that asks a different question.</p>

<p>IVF asks: <em>How can we achieve a pregnancy?</em></p>
<p>RRM asks: <em>Why can't this couple conceive, and can we fix it?</em></p>

<p>These are fundamentally different starting points. They lead to different evaluations, different treatments, and different outcomes for the couple's overall health.</p>

<p>Much of conventional fertility care operates on an unspoken assumption: <em>your body is broken, nature has failed, and technology must take over.</em> RRM challenges that assumption. In most cases, the body is not broken. It is sick, undiagnosed, or mistreated. When the disease is identified and treated, reproductive function often returns on its own.</p>

<h3>The Cost Difference</h3>

<p>Cost is one of the starkest differences. Katz et al. (2011) found that the average cost of IVF is 20 times greater than medication-only fertility treatments. IVF cycles typically range from $15,000 to $30,000, and many couples require multiple cycles. RRM treatments, particularly medication-based protocols, use standard pharmaceutical agents (progesterone, letrozole, hCG) that are a fraction of IVF drug costs. Many RRM evaluations and treatments are coded as treatment for diagnosed medical conditions, making them eligible for insurance coverage that IVF often does not receive.</p>

<h3>Obstetric Outcomes</h3>

<p>RRM pregnancies are conceived naturally. They are almost exclusively singletons. This matters for obstetric outcomes. Multiple pregnancies carry significantly higher risks of preterm delivery, low birth weight, NICU admission, and maternal complications including preeclampsia and hemorrhage.</p>

<p>IVF pregnancies, particularly those involving multiple embryo transfer, carry elevated risks of multiple gestation. Even singleton IVF pregnancies are associated with higher rates of preterm delivery, low birth weight, and perinatal complications compared to spontaneously conceived singletons. RRM pregnancies do not carry these IVF-associated risks because conception occurs naturally after the underlying condition has been treated.</p>

<h3>The IVF Evidence Gap</h3>

<p>IVF is often presented as the gold standard of fertility treatment. The evidence base tells a more complicated story.</p>

<p><strong>IVF success rates have not meaningfully improved in over two decades.</strong> Peer-reviewed analysis of CDC surveillance data shows that for fresh non-donor cycles, the live birth rate peaked at approximately 30% around 2010 and then declined to 22% by 2016, levels not seen since the late 1990s. Recent headline figures showing improvement (37% in 2022) reflect a methodological shift toward freeze-all protocols and frozen embryo transfers, not a breakthrough in underlying per-retrieval productivity.</p>

<p><strong>For unexplained infertility, there is insufficient evidence that IVF outperforms expectant management.</strong> The 2023 Cochrane systematic review of IVF for unexplained subfertility (Sunkara et al.) found only two randomized controlled trials with a combined 86 women and concluded that reviewers were "unable to draw any firm conclusions" about whether IVF is associated with higher live birth rates than trying naturally. A companion 2019 Cochrane network meta-analysis (Wang et al.) reached the same conclusion: "insufficient evidence of differences in live birth between expectant management and IVF/ICSI." Despite this, IVF is routinely offered as a first-line intervention for unexplained infertility after minimal evaluation.</p>

<p><strong>No randomized controlled trial has compared IVF to non-IVF treatment in women with PCOS or endometriosis.</strong> The 2023 International Evidence-based PCOS Guideline (co-endorsed by major professional societies) explicitly states that it "did not identify any published RCTs comparing IVF treatment with ovulation induction treatments or placebo/no treatment in PCOS." Its IVF recommendation is classified as a Clinical Consensus Recommendation, not evidence-based. For endometriosis, the 2022 European endometriosis guideline acknowledges the same gap: "many clinical decision points have not been evaluated in RCTs." IVF use for the two most common reproductive conditions is based on clinical consensus and observational data, not RCT evidence of superiority over alternatives.</p>

<p>None of this means IVF does not work. It means the evidence base supporting IVF as the default pathway for these conditions is far weaker than patients are typically told. When a treatment lacks RCT evidence, that applies to IVF as much as it applies to RRM.</p>

<h3>RRM After Failed IVF</h3>

<p>RRM can help couples who have already undergone failed IVF cycles. Because IVF bypasses the underlying condition, that condition remains after IVF fails. RRM identifies and treats it.</p>

<p>Boyle et al. (2022) reported a case of successful pregnancy achieved through RRM after 16 years of infertility, three recurrent miscarriages, and eight unsuccessful embryo transfers with IVF/ICSI. The RRM evaluation identified treatable conditions that the IVF pathway had never investigated. After targeted treatment, the couple achieved natural conception and delivered a healthy singleton.</p>

<p>Boyle et al. (2018) published the most detailed study on this question in <em>Frontiers in Medicine</em>. Among 403 couples who had already failed an average of 2.1 IVF cycles, RRM achieved a life-table live birth rate of 32.1%. Of the 74 live births, only one was a twin (1.4%), and 92% were born at term (37+ weeks). These results demonstrate that RRM is not simply an alternative to IVF. It finds and treats conditions that IVF never investigates. For couples who have been told "IVF is your only option" and it has not worked, RRM offers a fundamentally different path.</p>

<h3>When IVF May Be Appropriate</h3>

<p>RRM cannot help every couple. Bilateral tubal occlusion (both fallopian tubes are blocked or absent) prevents egg transport regardless of hormonal health. Severe male factor infertility, such as azoospermia (no sperm in the ejaculate), may require assisted reproduction when no treatable cause can be identified. When time is a critical factor and the couple makes a fully informed choice, IVF may be the right path.</p>

<p>Honest medicine requires acknowledging these limits. RRM physicians do so. What distinguishes RRM is the insistence that treatable conditions should be treated first, not bypassed, and that couples deserve a thorough evaluation before being directed to procedures that do not address their underlying health.</p>

<h3>A Note on IVF Outcome Reporting</h3>

<p>In the United States, SART membership is voluntary, and SART serves as the primary data intermediary for the CDC's National ART Surveillance System (NASS). A 2013 analysis in <em>Fertility and Sterility</em> found that 13 clinics (3.8% of U.S. fertility centers) accounted for half of all cycles excluded from SART reporting; those clinics published above-average success rates and grew their market share by 19.9%. HFEA data from the United Kingdom provides a more comprehensive picture because reporting is mandatory for all licensed clinics. HFEA data shows an average live birth rate of approximately 33% per embryo transferred for women under 35, with rates declining significantly with age: approximately 25% for ages 35-37, 17% for ages 38-39, and under 10% for women over 42.</p>

<h2 id="rrm-vs-ivf">RRM and IVF: A Detailed Comparison</h2>

<p>Restorative reproductive medicine spans the entire reproductive lifespan. It applies to adolescents with painful periods, women with PCOS managing metabolic health, couples navigating recurrent miscarriage, individuals seeking answers for hormonal disorders, and women in the perimenopausal transition. Most of what RRM treats has nothing to do with IVF. Endometriosis does not become a concern only when someone wants to conceive. Hormonal imbalances matter at every stage. The menstrual cycle is a diagnostic window across four decades of a woman's life.</p>

<p>That said, for couples navigating infertility, the comparison matters. IVF is the default recommendation in most fertility clinics. Many couples encounter it before they have had a thorough diagnostic workup. Understanding what RRM offers in this context, and how the two approaches differ, is essential information for anyone trying to make a genuinely informed decision.</p>

<h3>The Fundamental Difference</h3>

<p>IVF asks: how do we achieve pregnancy? It retrieves eggs, fertilizes them in a laboratory, and transfers embryos. The underlying condition, whatever caused the infertility, remains. IVF works around it.</p>

<p>RRM asks: what is causing this, and can we treat it? The diagnostic process identifies the specific conditions affecting each partner. Treatment addresses those conditions directly. If successful, the couple conceives naturally because the underlying condition has been treated, not bypassed.</p>

<p>This is not a philosophical difference. It is a clinical one. A couple with undiagnosed endometriosis, luteal phase deficiency, and subclinical male factor does not have "unexplained infertility." They have three undiagnosed conditions. IVF bypasses all three. RRM investigates and treats all three. These are not parallel paths to the same destination. They are different paradigms of care.</p>

<p>"Unexplained infertility" is not a diagnosis. It is a documentation that the workup was incomplete. In Boyle et al. (2025), the proportion of couples labeled "unexplained" dropped from 24% to 1% after RRM evaluation.<sup><a href="#ref-24" id="cite-24g">24</a></sup> The conditions were there. They had not been found.</p>

<h3>The Cost Difference</h3>

<p>A single IVF cycle in the United States costs $15,000 to $30,000, including medications and monitoring. Most couples require two to three cycles. Average total out-of-pocket costs reach $40,000 to $60,000, and can rise substantially with add-ons such as preimplantation genetic testing (PGT-A), intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI), and frozen embryo transfers. In the United States, a minority of states mandate insurance coverage for IVF, leaving most couples to pay out of pocket.<sup><a href="#ref-15" id="cite-15b">15</a></sup></p>

<p>RRM treats diagnosed medical conditions. The diagnostic workup, hormonal panels, charting instruction, targeted medications, and surgery each carry standard CPT codes that insurance already covers for their clinical indications. Endometriosis surgery is coded as a gynecologic procedure. Thyroid evaluation is coded as endocrine care. Luteal phase support is coded as hormonal treatment. This structural advantage makes RRM accessible to couples who cannot afford IVF's cash-pay model. Total RRM costs depend on the complexity of the diagnosis, but they represent a fraction of what IVF typically costs.</p>

<h3>Obstetric Outcomes</h3>

<p>RRM pregnancies are naturally conceived and are almost exclusively singletons. This distinction carries direct clinical significance.</p>

<p>Multiple gestation pregnancies, which occur at higher rates with IVF, particularly with multiple embryo transfer, carry elevated risks of preterm delivery, low birth weight, cerebral palsy, and neonatal death. Even singleton IVF pregnancies carry higher rates of adverse obstetric outcomes compared to spontaneously conceived singletons. HFEA data documents higher rates of preterm birth, low birth weight, and perinatal complications in IVF-conceived pregnancies.<sup><a href="#ref-16" id="cite-16g">16</a></sup> Bewley et al. (2011) documented increased rates of ectopic pregnancy, preeclampsia, placenta previa, and cesarean delivery associated with IVF.<sup><a href="#ref-19" id="cite-19b">19</a></sup></p>

<p>Boyle et al. (2025) reported a singleton preterm birth rate of 4.0% in RRM pregnancies, compared to 11.8% in CDC population data and 14.4% in SART-reported IVF singletons.<sup><a href="#ref-24" id="cite-24h">24</a></sup> The multiple pregnancy rate in the same cohort was 2.5%, compared to 6-7% in IVF data.</p>

<p>These differences reflect a structural reality. When the underlying condition is treated and the couple conceives naturally, the pregnancy is not procedure-associated. The risks specific to embryo transfer, ovarian hyperstimulation, and laboratory handling of gametes do not apply. The mother's health is also improved because the condition driving the dysfunction has been addressed, reducing complications from untreated endometriosis, hormonal deficiency, or inflammatory disease during pregnancy.<sup><a href="#ref-25" id="cite-25b">25</a></sup></p>

<h3>The IVF Evidence Gap</h3>

<p>IVF is practiced at enormous scale. The published volume on IVF is vast. What the evidence base contains, and what it lacks, are both worth understanding.</p>

<p>IVF has never been tested in a randomized controlled trial against expectant management for couples with unexplained infertility. The 2023 Cochrane review on IVF for unexplained subfertility identified only two relevant RCTs, covering a combined total of 86 women.<sup><a href="#ref-33" id="cite-33">33</a></sup> The 2019 Cochrane network meta-analysis on interventions for unexplained infertility found insufficient evidence to rank treatment options by efficacy.<sup><a href="#ref-34" id="cite-34">34</a></sup></p>

<p>No RCT has tested IVF versus active diagnosis and treatment for couples with endometriosis or PCOS. The 2023 International Evidence-based Guideline for PCOS Assessment and Management acknowledged this gap explicitly.<sup><a href="#ref-35" id="cite-35b">35</a></sup> The 2022 ESHRE endometriosis guideline similarly lacks an RCT comparing IVF to surgical and hormonal treatment of the underlying disease.</p>

<p>IVF success rates have also declined over time in the United States, despite continued expansion in the use of add-ons and adjunctive technologies. CDC surveillance data shows that fresh non-donor live birth rates fell from approximately 30% in 2010 to 22% in 2016, even as per-cycle costs rose.<sup><a href="#ref-31" id="cite-31c">31</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-32" id="cite-32b">32</a></sup></p>

<p>None of this means IVF does not work for specific indications. It means the evidentiary standard applied to IVF is not as rigorous as commonly assumed, and that the call for more RCTs applies equally to both paradigms.</p>

<h3>RRM After Failed IVF</h3>

<p>A recurring pattern in RRM practice is couples who arrive after multiple unsuccessful IVF cycles. The prior workup did not identify why conception was not occurring. IVF was attempted without a diagnosis. It did not succeed. The couple had a procedure but still did not have an answer.</p>

<p>Boyle et al. (2018) published a cohort study in <em>Frontiers in Medicine</em> following 403 couples who had undergone an average of 2.1 prior IVF attempts without success. After RRM evaluation and treatment, the life-table live birth rate was 32.1%. Of 74 live births, only 1 was a twin (1.4%). 92% of births occurred at 37 weeks or later.<sup><a href="#ref-17" id="cite-17d">17</a></sup></p>

<p>Boyle et al. (2022) published a case report in the <em>Journal of Medical Case Reports</em> documenting natural conception after 16 years of infertility, three recurrent miscarriages, and eight failed IVF embryo transfers. RRM evaluation identified underlying conditions that had not been diagnosed during the IVF process. The couple conceived naturally after treatment.<sup><a href="#ref-10" id="cite-10d">10</a></sup></p>

<p>These cases share a common thread. IVF did not fail because the technology malfunctioned. It was not designed to address the underlying conditions. RRM did not succeed despite those conditions. It succeeded because it identified and treated them. What looks like "IVF failure" is often, on closer evaluation, a diagnostic gap that no one had investigated.</p>

<h2 id="cost-insurance">How Much Does RRM Cost?</h2>

<p>Cost is one of the most common questions couples ask. The answer matters because it shapes who can access care.</p>

<p>IVF costs $15,000 to $30,000 per cycle in the United States, including medications and monitoring. Most couples need two to three cycles to achieve a live birth, bringing average total out-of-pocket spending to $40,000 to $60,000. Costs rise substantially for older patients and with add-ons like genetic testing (PGT-A), ICSI, and frozen embryo transfers. The Katz et al. prospective cohort study found that IVF costs on average 20 times more than medication-based fertility treatments.</p>

<p>RRM costs vary by diagnosis and treatment plan. The key structural advantage is that RRM treats diagnosed medical conditions using standard diagnostic and treatment codes. Charting instruction, hormonal panels, medications, and surgery for conditions like endometriosis or PCOS use the same CPT codes any specialist would bill. This means many components of RRM care are covered by standard health insurance, without requiring a separate "fertility treatment" benefit. By contrast, IVF is typically classified as elective fertility treatment and covered by insurance in only a minority of U.S. states, leaving most couples to pay out of pocket.</p>

<h3>Insurance Coverage</h3>

<p>Many RRM treatments are covered by insurance because they are coded as treatment for diagnosed medical conditions, not as "fertility treatment." Endometriosis surgery uses standard surgical codes. Thyroid treatment uses endocrine codes. PCOS management uses metabolic and gynecologic codes. The diagnostic workup uses the same CPT codes any specialist would bill. This is a significant advantage. When a condition has a diagnostic code, insurance pays for its treatment regardless of whether the patient is also trying to conceive.</p>

<h3>Legislative Progress</h3>

<p>The policy landscape is shifting. In 2025, Arkansas became the first state to mandate insurance coverage for restorative reproductive medicine. The RESTORE Act (H.R. 3589), introduced in the 119th Congress, seeks to expand federal access to RRM as an alternative to IVF coverage mandates. The national debate over IVF policy, including the Trump administration's February 2025 executive order on IVF access, has created new visibility for cause-based approaches and raised the question of whether they deserve equal standing in coverage mandates.</p>

<h3>What to Ask Your Insurer</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Ask about diagnostic coverage.</strong> "Is cycle-timed hormonal testing covered under my plan when ordered for a diagnosed condition such as PCOS, endometriosis, or luteal phase defect?"</li>
  <li><strong>Ask about surgical coverage.</strong> "Is laparoscopic excision surgery for endometriosis covered as a gynecologic procedure, separate from any fertility benefit?"</li>
  <li><strong>Ask about out-of-network benefits.</strong> Many RRM physicians are not in-network for every plan. Out-of-network benefits may still cover a significant portion of costs.</li>
  <li><strong>Ask about your state's mandates.</strong> Some states require coverage for fertility diagnosis or treatment. RRM treatments often qualify under these mandates because they treat medical conditions, not just infertility as a standalone diagnosis.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="cost-insurance">How Much Does RRM Cost?</h2>

<p>RRM treats diagnosed medical conditions. Endometriosis has a diagnostic code. PCOS has a diagnostic code. Luteal phase deficiency has a diagnostic code. Hormonal disorders, cycle irregularities, recurrent pregnancy loss, and perimenopausal transitions all have diagnostic codes. This is not a technicality. It is the clinical and financial foundation of how RRM care is accessed.</p>

<p>Standard health insurance covers the treatment of diagnosed conditions. That coverage applies whether the patient is trying to conceive, managing painful periods, investigating irregular cycles, or addressing a hormonal disorder at any stage of the reproductive lifespan. RRM's diagnostic framework works within that system rather than outside it.</p>

<h3>Insurance Coverage</h3>

<p>Many RRM treatments are billable under standard insurance codes because they treat specific diagnosed conditions.</p>

<p>Endometriosis excision uses surgical codes for laparoscopic gynecologic procedures. Thyroid evaluation and treatment use standard endocrine codes. PCOS management uses metabolic and gynecologic codes. Hormonal testing across the menstrual cycle, when ordered for a diagnosed condition, uses the same laboratory codes any specialist would order. Progesterone supplementation for luteal phase deficiency is a standard hormonal medication. Semen analysis is a standard diagnostic test. None of these require a separate "fertility treatment" benefit to be covered.</p>
