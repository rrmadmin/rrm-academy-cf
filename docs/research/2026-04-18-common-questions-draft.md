## rec-01 (P5) What is restorative reproductive medicine (RRM)?

### HTML block

```html
<!-- rec-01 -->
<h2>What is restorative reproductive medicine (RRM)?</h2>

<p>
  Most fertility medicine asks one question: can we get you pregnant? RRM asks a different question first.
  Why isn't it happening? The distinction is not semantic. It changes everything about how a patient is evaluated and treated.
</p>

<div class="evidence-block">
  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    Restorative Reproductive Medicine is a medical discipline that identifies and treats the underlying
    conditions behind reproductive health problems rather than suppressing symptoms or bypassing the
    reproductive system entirely. It uses the Creighton Model FertilityCare System as a real-time
    diagnostic tool: daily charting of cervical mucus and cycle biomarkers gives clinicians the same
    kind of prospective data that a cardiologist gets from continuous monitoring. What gets measured
    gets treated. Conditions diagnosed and treated under RRM include endometriosis, PCOS, recurrent
    pregnancy loss, luteal phase deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, tubal factor, male factor, and
    premature ovarian insufficiency, among others. When a cause is found and treated, conception
    happens the way the body was designed for it to happen.
  </p>
  <p>
    RRM is not a step toward IVF. It is not a complement to IVF. It is a separate paradigm: treat the
    cause, restore function, and support the couple in achieving pregnancy naturally. Published
    outcomes support this approach. In a five-year cohort of 1,310 infertile couples, RRM achieved a
    50% take-home baby rate at 24 months and 62.1% at 36 months or more
    (Sánchez-Méndez JI et al., <em>Frontiers in Reproductive Health</em>, 2025). All pregnancies
    through natural conception. No IVF.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>
    "Unexplained infertility" is not a diagnosis. It is the absence of one. RRM exists to find what
    was missed. Our courses train patients and clinicians to ask better questions before accepting
    that the body has failed.
  </p>
</div>
```

## rec-02 (P5) How does restorative reproductive medicine work?

### HTML block

```html
<!-- rec-02 -->
<h2>How does restorative reproductive medicine work?</h2>

<div class="evidence-block">
  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    RRM works in three stages: observe, diagnose, treat.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Stage 1: Observe.</strong> The patient learns the Creighton Model FertilityCare System
    from a certified FertilityCare Practitioner (FCP). She charts daily biomarkers including cervical
    mucus patterns, cycle length, and hormonal indicators. This produces a real-time picture of her
    reproductive physiology. A trained RRM physician reads that chart the way a cardiologist reads an
    ECG: as a live data stream, not a historical summary. Practicing without it means flying blind.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Stage 2: Diagnose.</strong> Using the chart alongside targeted lab work and imaging,
    the clinician identifies the root cause. Luteal phase deficiency. Endometriosis. Thyroid
    dysfunction. Low progesterone. Elevated androgens in PCOS. Structural tubal disease. Male factor
    abnormalities. These are diagnosable conditions, not mysteries. RRM clinicians evaluate both
    partners from the first appointment.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Stage 3: Treat.</strong> Treatment is directed at the identified cause. Endometriosis is
    excised, not suppressed. Hormonal deficiencies are corrected with cycle-timed protocols.
    Structural problems are addressed surgically. Male factor is treated directly. Charting continues
    through treatment so clinicians can track the response in real time and adjust.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="acknowledge-block">
  <h3 class="acknowledge-label">What we acknowledge</h3>
  <p>
    Stage 1 requires learning. It takes one to three months to produce a chart that is diagnostically
    useful. That investment is the entry point for everything that follows. For some couples,
    particularly those with age-related urgency, that timeline deserves direct discussion with their
    clinician.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>
    The chart is the tool that makes cause-based medicine possible. Without it, clinicians are
    evaluating a snapshot. With it, they have a longitudinal record of how the reproductive system
    is actually functioning. That is a different kind of clinical data, and it leads to different
    clinical decisions.
  </p>
</div>
```

## rec-03 (P5) What conditions does RRM treat?

### HTML block

```html
<!-- rec-03 -->
<h2>What conditions does RRM treat?</h2>

<p>
  RRM is not an infertility program with a narrower menu. It is a full-spectrum approach to
  reproductive health. The same diagnostic framework that finds the cause of infertility also
  finds the cause of painful periods, irregular cycles, mood disorders tied to the cycle, and
  pregnancy loss. These are not separate specialties in RRM. They are the same question asked
  at different life stages.
</p>

<div class="evidence-block">
  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    Conditions diagnosed and treated under RRM include:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Endometriosis</strong> (excision surgery, adhesion prevention, hormonal support)</li>
    <li><strong>Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)</strong> (ovulatory dysfunction, androgen excess)</li>
    <li><strong>Recurrent pregnancy loss</strong> (luteal phase deficiency, thyroid, structural)</li>
    <li><strong>Infertility</strong> (all etiologies, both partners)</li>
    <li><strong>Luteal phase deficiency (LPD)</strong> (cycle-timed progesterone protocols)</li>
    <li><strong>Thyroid dysfunction</strong> (subclinical hypothyroidism impairs implantation)</li>
    <li><strong>Premature ovarian insufficiency</strong></li>
    <li><strong>Tubal factor</strong> (selective HSG, salpingography)</li>
    <li><strong>Male factor infertility</strong> (evaluation and referral for restorative andrology)</li>
    <li><strong>Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD)</strong></li>
    <li><strong>Abnormal uterine bleeding</strong></li>
    <li><strong>Ovarian cysts</strong></li>
    <li><strong>Postpartum depression</strong></li>
    <li><strong>Perimenopause</strong></li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    The menstrual cycle is a vital sign. Major professional societies affirm this. When the cycle is
    disrupted, something is wrong. RRM treats the condition causing the disruption, not just the
    disruption itself.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>
    Women diagnosed with "unexplained infertility" have not been told there is no cause. They have
    been told their clinician did not find one. That is a different statement. RRM starts where that
    workup stopped.
  </p>
</div>
```

## rec-04 (P5) Does RRM treat male infertility?

### HTML block

```html
<!-- rec-04 -->
<h2>Does RRM treat male infertility?</h2>

<p>
  Infertility is a couple's diagnosis, not a woman's. The data have been consistent for decades.
  Male factor is solely responsible in roughly 20% of couples experiencing infertility and
  contributes in an additional 30 to 40% (Male Infertility, StatPearls, NCBI Bookshelf, 2024; consistent with AUA/ASRM Guideline, 2024, DOI: 10.1097/JU.0000000000004180).
  Evaluating only the woman is not a workup. It is half a workup.
</p>

<div class="evidence-block">
  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    RRM evaluates both partners from the first clinical appointment. For the male partner, this
    typically includes a detailed semen analysis (volume, concentration, motility, morphology,
    DNA fragmentation where indicated), hormonal assessment (testosterone, FSH, LH), and
    review of lifestyle and environmental factors known to affect spermatogenesis.
  </p>
  <p>
    Where structural or hormonal causes of male factor are identified, RRM clinicians refer for
    restorative andrology: varicocele repair, hormonal correction, or treatment of contributing
    conditions rather than bypassing sperm quality through intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI).
    The goal is to improve the underlying sperm parameters so the couple can conceive naturally.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="acknowledge-block">
  <h3 class="acknowledge-label">What we acknowledge</h3>
  <p>
    Severe male factor (very low sperm counts, azoospermia) may present a genuine clinical
    challenge for natural conception. RRM clinicians should give couples direct, honest counseling
    about what the data show and what the realistic options are when a restorative approach has
    been optimized.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>
    A fertility evaluation that does not include the male partner is incomplete. RRM treats both.
    That is not a policy position. That is clinical accuracy.
  </p>
</div>
```

## rec-05 (P5) What kind of doctor provides RRM / NaProTechnology care?

### HTML block

```html
<!-- rec-05 -->
<h2>What kind of doctor provides RRM / NaProTechnology care?</h2>

<div class="evidence-block">
  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    RRM and NaProTechnology care involves two distinct roles. Understanding the difference helps
    patients find the right entry point.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>FertilityCare Practitioners (FCPs):</strong> These are educators, not physicians.
    They teach the Creighton Model FertilityCare System: how to chart cervical biomarkers and
    read the cycle. This is where most patients begin. FCPs are certified through FertilityCare
    Centers of America (FCCA) after a supervised training program. Some are nurses or allied
    health professionals; many are lay practitioners. They do not prescribe medications or
    perform procedures.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>NaPro-trained physicians:</strong> These are licensed medical doctors (MD or DO)
    who complete specialized training in NaProTechnology through programs including the Pope Paul VI
    Institute fellowship in Omaha, Nebraska, or accredited international equivalents. Credentialing
    includes the NaProTechnology Medical Consultancy (NFPMC) designation. These physicians read
    Creighton charts, interpret hormone profiles, perform RRM-indicated surgeries (including
    excision for endometriosis), and manage medical protocols. Many are OBGYNs; some are family
    physicians or reproductive endocrinologists who completed NaPro training.
  </p>
  <p>
    To find a trained provider, RRM Academy maintains a practitioner directory at
    <a href="/find-a-provider/">rrmacademy.org/find-a-provider</a>.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="acknowledge-block">
  <h3 class="acknowledge-label">What we acknowledge</h3>
  <p>
    The number of trained RRM clinicians is smaller than the demand in many regions, particularly
    outside the United States, Ireland, Spain, and Poland. Telehealth consultations have expanded
    access, but surgical care still requires proximity to a trained surgeon.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>
    Training more clinicians is one of the reasons RRM Academy exists. Our courses serve
    practitioners seeking to understand and integrate RRM into their practice.
  </p>
```

## rec-06 (P5) How much does RRM cost, and is it covered by insurance?

### HTML block

```html
<!-- rec-06 -->
<h2>How much does RRM cost, and is it covered by insurance?</h2>

<p>
  Cost is one of the most common reasons couples investigate RRM. The short answer: RRM is
  substantially less expensive than IVF, and most of the clinical components are billed through
  standard insurance codes.
</p>

<div class="evidence-block">
  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    RRM does not have a single price tag because it is not a single procedure. It is a diagnostic
    and treatment process, and the cost depends on what is found and what needs to be treated.
    The major cost components:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>
      <strong>FertilityCare instruction:</strong> A multi-session introductory series with a certified
      FertilityCare Practitioner. Fees vary by center and are set individually by each FCCA-affiliated
      practice. Not usually covered by insurance. Cost is typically a fraction of a single specialist
      consult sequence.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Physician consultations:</strong> Billed using standard CPT codes for office visits
      (99213, 99214, 99215) and gynecology evaluation. Covered by most major insurance plans
      the same as any OBGYN visit.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Diagnostic labs:</strong> Hormone assays (progesterone, estrogen, FSH, LH, thyroid,
      androgens) billed under standard lab CPT codes. Usually covered under diagnostic benefit.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Diagnostic imaging:</strong> Ultrasound, selective HSG. Covered under diagnostic
      imaging benefit when medically indicated.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Surgery:</strong> Excision surgery for endometriosis, adhesiolysis, tubal
      procedures. Billed under CPT codes for laparoscopic gynecologic surgery. Covered by
      insurance when medically indicated. Prior authorization required in most plans.
    </li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    IVF, by contrast, typically costs $15,000 to $25,000 per cycle when medications and monitoring
    are included, and most couples require more than one cycle. Total cumulative costs across multiple
    attempts rise substantially, and IVF is excluded from coverage by the majority of insurance plans
    in the United States. Some states mandate IVF coverage; RRM procedures are almost universally
    covered as standard medical care regardless of state mandate status.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="acknowledge-block">
  <h3 class="acknowledge-label">What we acknowledge</h3>
  <p>
    Insurance coverage for RRM-adjacent services varies by plan. Some insurers require referrals
    or pre-authorization for specialist consults. NaPro-specific medications (such as
    cycle-timed progesterone) may require prior authorization or be subject to formulary
    restrictions. Patients should verify their specific plan's coverage for gynecologic surgery
    and reproductive endocrine evaluation before beginning.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>
    The cost comparison matters because it changes the conversation. When a diagnosed condition
    is treated with standard medical care, insurance covers it. When the reproductive system is
    bypassed instead of treated, the patient pays out of pocket. RRM treats the cause. That
    treatment is insurable.
  </p>
</div>
```

## rec-07 (P4) Is RRM effective? Does it actually work?

### HTML block

```html
<!-- rec-07 -->
<h2>Is RRM effective? Does it actually work?</h2>

<p>
  This is the right question to ask. Here is what the published data show.
</p>

<div class="evidence-block">
  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    Two landmark studies published in 2025 provide the strongest current data on RRM outcomes.
  </p>
  <p>
    The first followed 1,310 infertile couples treated with NaProTechnology at a single fertility
    clinic in Spain over five years. The take-home baby rate was <strong>50% at 24 months</strong>
    and <strong>62.1% at 36 months or more</strong>. All pregnancies through natural conception.
    The population included couples with endometriosis, PCOS, recurrent pregnancy loss, and
    unexplained infertility (Sánchez-Méndez JI et al., <em>Frontiers in Reproductive Health</em>, 2025).
  </p>
  <p>
    The second was the first direct head-to-head comparison of RRM outcomes against IVF registry
    data. In a retrospective cohort of 187 couples (average female age 36.4, average time trying
    32 months), the crude live birth rate with RRM treatment was <strong>41%</strong>, comparable
    to IVF live birth rates for the same age group in the HFEA registry
    (Boyle P et al., <em>Journal of Restorative Reproductive Medicine</em>, 2025).
    All pregnancies through natural conception.
  </p>
  <p>
    For specific conditions, the evidence is stronger. Excision surgery for endometriosis shows a
    repeat surgery rate of 2.5% over ten years
    (Yeung P et al., <em>Preprints.org</em>, 2024), compared to historical ablation recurrence
    rates above 40%. Treating the disease, not masking it, changes the long-term trajectory.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="acknowledge-block">
  <h3 class="acknowledge-label">What we acknowledge</h3>
  <p>
    Most RRM outcomes studies are retrospective and single-center. Randomized controlled trials
    comparing RRM directly to IVF do not yet exist for this population. The evidence base is growing
    rapidly, but it is not yet the volume that some researchers require before changing practice.
    RRM Academy presents the evidence accurately, including its limitations.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>
    IVF was adopted into widespread practice before it met the RCT standard now demanded of RRM.
    Cochrane's own 2023 review found only two randomized controlled trials with 86 women total
    comparing IVF to expectant management. The evidentiary standard applied to RRM is not
    consistently applied to IVF. The 2025 data are meaningful. Patients deserve to know they exist.
  </p>
</div>
```

```html
<h2>Is RRM a real alternative to IVF?</h2>

<p>
  The framing of this question matters. RRM is not a workaround for couples who cannot afford IVF,

</p>

  <h3 class="evidence-label">The evidence</h3>
  <p>
    IVF is a bypass. It extracts eggs, fertilizes them outside the body, and transfers embryos
    into the uterus. The underlying condition that caused infertility is not treated. The
    endometriosis is still there. The luteal phase defect is still there. The hormonal dysfunction
    is still there. IVF reroutes around the problem.
  </p>
  <p>
    RRM treats the problem. When a couple completes RRM successfully, the woman's reproductive
    health is in better condition than when she started. That improvement persists. Subsequent
    pregnancies become more accessible, not less.
  </p>
  <p>
    The published outcomes are comparable. In a 2025 head-to-head evaluation, 187 couples treated
    with RRM achieved a 41% crude live birth rate, all through natural conception, benchmarked
    against IVF registry data for the same age group
    (Boyle P et al., <em>Journal of Restorative Reproductive Medicine</em>, 2025).
    A separate five-year cohort of 1,310 couples showed 50% at 24 months and 62.1% at 36 months
    or more (Sánchez-Méndez JI et al., <em>Frontiers in Reproductive Health</em>, 2025).
  </p>
  <p>
    The difference is what happens to the patient's body. IVF carries documented risks: ovarian
    hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS), which is associated with a two-fold increase in preterm
    delivery before 34 weeks (Asfour V et al., <em>J Obstet Gynaecol Isr</em>, 2021, PMID: 34327685),
    and elevated rates of prematurity even in singleton IVF pregnancies. The HFEA reports that
    in the early 1990s, around 28% of IVF births were multiples, compared to an estimated 1 to 2%
    in natural conception (HFEA, <em>Fertility Treatment 2023: Trends and Figures</em>).
    RRM treats the condition using standard surgical and medical interventions. The risks are those
    of the specific treatment, not of reproductive bypass.
  </p>
</div>

  <h3 class="acknowledge-label">What we acknowledge</h3>
  <p>
    For some couples, particularly those with age-related urgency, very low ovarian reserve, or
    severe azoospermia, the restorative pathway may not produce the same probability of pregnancy
    within the same timeframe. RRM clinicians should give these couples direct and honest
    information about what the data show for their specific situation. The goal of informed
    consent is not to steer. It is to inform.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="position-block">
  <h3 class="position-label">RRM Academy's position</h3>
  <p>

    take over. RRM challenges that. Not because technology is bad, but because the premise is
    often wrong. Most couples with "unexplained infertility" have a diagnosable, treatable
    condition. Finding it first is not a detour. It is the point.
  </p>
</div>
```
