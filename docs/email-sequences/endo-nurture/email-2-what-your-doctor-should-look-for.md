## Body

Your PDF has been with you for three days now.

If you circled anything, those symptoms are not random. They are a clinical pattern. The question is whether the clinician you see next will read them that way.

Most won't. Not because they don't care. Because the standard workup doesn't require it.

Here is what "standard" typically looks like: a pelvic exam, a few questions, and a prescription for hormonal contraception. "See how you feel in a few months." That approach treats the symptom as the problem. It does not ask what is causing it.

Here is what a thorough reproductive workup looks like: charting your cycle to identify your actual hormonal pattern, timed hormone panels drawn across specific days, not just a single lab on a random Tuesday. Pelvic ultrasound interpreted alongside your cycle data. A full picture before a recommendation is made. When surgical evaluation is indicated, the question is not just whether disease is present, but what kind, where, and what approach is appropriate.

One approach suppresses the signal. The other reads it.

More than [X] women have used this survey. Most of them have already seen a clinician. Many have already been told their symptoms are "normal" or were offered suppressive medications as the only next step. The survey exists because that answer is not sufficient for a real diagnosis.

Whether you took this for yourself or for someone you care for, the next few emails will give you the vocabulary for a different kind of appointment.

**Your action for this week:** Open your PDF and write down the three symptoms that concern you most. Those become your conversation starters. Specific symptoms get specific questions. Specific questions get diagnostic answers.

Understanding your clinical pattern is step one. Knowing what kind of clinician to look for is step two. More on that soon.

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI
