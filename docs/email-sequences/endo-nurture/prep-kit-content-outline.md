## Section 1: Questions to Ask Your Doctor (4 pages, ~2,000 words)

One page per stage. Each question gets blank space below for writing the doctor's answer. One-sentence "why it matters" note under each question.

### Stage 1: Undiagnosed (8 questions)

1. "I have been tracking my symptoms for [X months]. Can we review my symptom log together right now?"
2. "What specific diagnostic steps are you recommending, and what is the clinical reasoning for each?"
3. "Are you ordering hormone panels timed to specific points in my cycle, or a single random blood draw?"
4. "Would a diagnostic laparoscopy be appropriate at this point, and if not, what criteria would trigger it?"
5. "If we do imaging, will the ultrasound be interpreted in the context of where I am in my cycle?"
6. "Have you considered having me chart my cycles using a standardized method as part of the workup?"
7. "What is your approach if initial tests come back 'normal' but my symptoms persist?"
8. "Are there medications you would recommend at this stage, and would any of them suppress symptoms in a way that delays diagnosis?"

### Stage 2: Newly Diagnosed (7 questions)

1. "What stage and location is my endometriosis, and can you show me the surgical photos or imaging?"
2. "Are you recommending excision or ablation, and what is your reasoning?"
3. "How many excision surgeries do you perform per year, and what is your complication rate?"
4. "What is your plan for investigating the underlying hormonal environment?"
5. "If you are recommending medication, is the goal to suppress symptoms or treat an identified dysfunction?"
6. "What does your follow-up plan look like for the next 6 to 12 months?"
7. "Are there co-occurring conditions we should screen for?"

### Stage 3: Pre-Surgical (7 questions)

1. "Will you be performing excision or ablation? If ablation, why not excision?"
2. "Will you use electrocautery or cold excision techniques, and what is your rationale?"
3. "What adhesion prevention barriers will you use, and at what point during surgery?"
4. "If you find disease beyond what imaging showed, will you excise all visible disease?"
5. "What is your plan for documenting findings? Will I receive photos and a detailed operative report?"
6. "What is your post-operative monitoring plan for recurrence prevention?"
7. "How long is recovery, and what symptoms should prompt me to call vs. go to the ER?"

### Stage 4: Post-Surgical (8 questions)

1. "Can we review my surgical photos and operative report together?"
2. "What is your plan for monitoring whether endometriosis is recurring?"
3. "Are you recommending post-surgical suppression? What is the specific goal and duration?"
4. "Can we do a full hormonal workup now to evaluate underlying reproductive function?"
5. "Should I be charting my cycles post-surgery, and will you use that data?"
6. "What would warrant a second surgery, and how would we make that decision?"
7. "Are there pelvic floor, nutritional, or other supportive therapies I should pursue?"
8. "If symptoms return, what is the diagnostic plan before assuming recurrence?"

## Section 3: Red Flags Checklist (1 page, two-column)

**Header:** "After Your Appointment: What Just Happened?"

### Signs You Are Being Dismissed (left column, red-tinted)

- Doctor did not look at your symptom log
- Immediately prescribed birth control without diagnostic workup
- Said "labs are normal" without ordering timed hormone panels
- Told you pelvic pain is "just part of being a woman"
- Dismissed your pain severity
- Refused to discuss diagnostic laparoscopy
- Recommended suppression without explaining what it suppresses
- No follow-up appointment scheduled
- Could not explain excision vs ablation
- Told you to "just get pregnant" as treatment

### Signs You Have a Good Doctor (right column, green-tinted)

- Reviewed your symptom log and asked follow-up questions
- Ordered timed hormone panels at specific cycle points
- Explained diagnostic options including surgical evaluation
- Clearly explained excision vs ablation with reasoning
- Discussed adhesion prevention strategies
- Was open to cycle charting as a diagnostic tool
- Created a specific follow-up plan with dates
- Acknowledged symptoms as real even if initial tests are normal
- Willing to refer to a specialist if outside their expertise
- Discussed underlying hormonal environment, not just lesions

**Footer:** "One red flag does not mean you have a bad doctor. A pattern of red flags means you need a different doctor."

## Section 4: Standard vs Thorough Workup (1 page)

**Header:** "Two Approaches to Endometriosis"

Comparison table:

| Area | Standard | Thorough Workup |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| Symptom history | Brief verbal report | Structured log reviewed over 1-2 cycles |
| Hormone testing | Single random blood draw | Timed panels at follicular, periovulatory, luteal |
| Cycle monitoring | Not discussed | Standardized charting as ongoing diagnostic data |
| Imaging | Ultrasound without cycle context | Ultrasound interpreted by cycle phase |
| Medication | Hormonal suppression as first-line | Targeted treatment of identified dysfunction |
| Surgery | Delayed; ablation common | Excision preferred; cold techniques considered |
| Adhesion prevention | Rarely discussed | Barriers used; patient informed pre-surgery |
| Post-surgical care | "Come back if you have pain" | Structured follow-up with hormonal re-evaluation |
| Underlying cause | Treated as surgical disease only | Investigated as hormonal/immunological/environmental |

**Closing:** "If the left column looks familiar, you are not alone. You are allowed to ask for the right column."
