## Body

You've probably heard some version of this: "Your pain is normal." "Let's try the pill and see if it helps." "Come back in six months."

The median time from first symptoms to endometriosis diagnosis is nine years. That figure comes from multiple systematic reviews. It has not meaningfully improved in decades.

Those nine years are not neutral time.

Suppressive medications mask symptoms. They do not reliably stop endometriosis lesions from advancing. The disease can continue to progress beneath the surface. In many cases, so can associated adhesion formation.

Here is what the research actually shows. A 1997 study by Dmowski and colleagues compared women presenting with pain to women presenting with infertility. The pain group waited an average of 6.35 years for a diagnosis. The infertility group waited 3.13 years. The pain group also had more advanced disease at diagnosis.

That is not coincidence. It reflects disease progression over time. Women with the most severe symptoms waited longest and arrived with the most advanced disease.

Every year of investigation that does not happen is a year the underlying condition may be advancing without anyone looking.

This matters for more than fertility. Adhesion formation associated with endometriosis can affect chronic pelvic pain, intercourse, and bowel function. A 2012 paper by Somigliana and colleagues described adhesion prevention in endometriosis as "a neglected critical challenge." Not a side issue. A central one.
