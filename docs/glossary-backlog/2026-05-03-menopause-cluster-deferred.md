**Gianna's draft (parked, not published):**

> The climacteric is an older clinical term for the broader transitional phase surrounding menopause, encompassing the years before and after the final menstrual period. It describes the full arc of ovarian aging rather than a single endpoint. Where perimenopause names the active transition and menopause names the confirmed cessation, climacteric encompasses both within a single frame.
>
> The term appears in older NaPro and gynecologic literature and clinicians still encounter it in published sources, though Perimenopause and Menopause now appear more commonly in both RRM and mainstream clinical writing.

**Why deferred:** Entry is clinically accurate from Hilgers Ch 45 framing (hot flashes as a marker that prompts hormonal evaluation; appearance under age 40 with elevated FSH/LH triggers POI workup). But it carries no epidemiologic data on prevalence or duration. Brian flagged he wanted to address this on a different date, possibly with a SWAN cohort or equivalent reference.

**To finish, need:**

- Decide whether to keep it brief (clinical-marker framing only) or expand to include patient-counseling-relevant prevalence data

**Gianna's draft (parked, not published):**

> Vasomotor symptoms are episodes of sudden heat sensation, flushing, and sweating that arise from hormonal fluctuations during the menopausal transition. Hot flashes are the most common form. They result from disrupted thermoregulatory signaling as estrogen levels decline and become less predictable. They occur during Perimenopause and may persist well beyond the final menstrual period.
>
> NaPro literature identifies hot flashes and vasomotor symptoms as clinical markers that prompt hormonal evaluation. When they appear in a woman under 40 alongside amenorrhea and elevated FSH and LH, clinicians evaluate for Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI). The presence and severity of vasomotor symptoms inform the individual clinical decision about whether hormonal support is appropriate.
