1,310 Couples. Five Years. One Clinic. A 62.1% Take-Home Baby Rate.

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

That number needs context. The study by Sanchez-Mendez et al. (2025, Frontiers in Reproductive Health) ran retrospectively across a 5-year cohort of infertile couples treated with NaProTechnology. More than a quarter of them had already failed conventional ART. Mean female age was 35. The median infertility duration was 24 months before they walked in.

The crude take-home baby rate was 35.3%. That is 463 live births in 1,310 couples. The actuarially adjusted cumulative rate, accounting for the median treatment duration of 10.9 months, reached 62.1%. These are not the same number dressed differently. The crude rate captures what the observation window could see. The adjusted rate models what the full cohort achieves given adequate time.

The age bands are the most clinically useful numbers in this paper.

83.7% adjusted THBR for women aged 18-30. 53.3% for ages 36-40. 24.4% for women over 40. A 38-year-old sitting across the desk asking "is it too late?" gets a real answer here, not a shrug toward the nearest ART clinic.

What the diagnoses tell us.

The average couple in this cohort carried 2.5 diagnosable conditions. Endometriosis, hormonal dysfunction, male factor, and endometrial disorders were each identified as independent positive predictors of a take-home baby. Read that again: having a diagnosis, including endometriosis, predicted better outcomes than having none. The mechanism is not mysterious. Diagnosis enables treatment. No diagnosis means treating nothing. What conventional medicine calls "unexplained infertility" is, in this cohort, a workup that has not been completed yet.

Recurrent pregnancy loss as the reason for consultation was also a positive predictor. These are not the easiest patients. They are the ones medicine has already sent away.

The male partner is in this data.

Mean male age was 36.9 years. Male factor appeared as an independent predictor of success. Both partners were evaluated and both were treated. Couples, not individual patients, are the unit of care.

Limitations worth naming.

This is a retrospective, single-center study. The adjusted cumulative rate depends on actuarial modeling and dropout assumptions. The authors conducted sensitivity analysis, which is appropriate, but the precise figure is model-dependent. There is no concurrent comparison arm. One clinic's outcomes reflect that clinic's protocols and surgical expertise. The 62.1% figure should be read as a real-world signal from a rigorous NaProTechnology center, not a universal guarantee.

What this adds to the evidence base.

This is the largest single-center NaProTechnology cohort published to date. Stanford, Parnell, and Boyle (2008) established baseline outcomes in an Irish general practice. Tham, Schliep, and Stanford (2012) replicated in a Canadian setting. De Groot et al. (2018) contributed European data. The 2025 Sanchez-Mendez study works with a harder population: older, longer-infertile, with prior ART failures in the denominator. The evidence base keeps growing. It keeps pointing the same direction.

Couples who have been told their infertility is unexplained have not been fully evaluated. The evaluation comes first. The treatment follows. The take-home baby is the result.

What to bring to your next appointment

  • Ask your provider how many diagnosable conditions they have evaluated for. The average in this study was 2.5 per couple.
  • If you have been told your infertility is "unexplained," ask specifically: have we looked at endometriosis, hormonal dysfunction, endometrial pathology, and male factor?
  • If you are over 35, know that age-stratified data exists. 53.3% cumulative THBR at 36-40 is not a reason to stop looking. It is a reason to start the diagnostic process now.
  • Ask whether both partners have been fully evaluated. Male factor was a positive predictor when treated.

Four in Ten Women Trying to Conceive Cannot Identify Their Own Fertile Window

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

You have probably been there. Months of trying, apps with conflicting predictions, and the creeping feeling that you are doing something wrong. Here is the thing: you are not doing something wrong. You were never taught.

A 2025 study in Reproductive Health surveyed 97,414 women in the UK who were actively trying to conceive. Forty-one percent of them could not accurately identify their fertile window. That is not a general population number. These are women who were already motivated enough to seek out a health assessment while trying for a pregnancy.

Irregular cycles compound the problem dramatically.

Women with irregular cycles had a fertile window awareness rate of just 26.8%, compared to 73.9% in women with regular cycles. Misidentifying your cycle as regular when it is not nearly tripled the odds of not knowing your fertile window (OR = 2.99). Nearly 1 in 4 women with irregular cycles had no idea when they were fertile.

The youngest women are the least informed.

Among women under 25, only 42.5% correctly identified their fertile window. Knowledge improved with age, reaching 67.1% in the 36-40 cohort, before dropping again after 45. Younger women who conceive later after prolonged trying will arrive in fertility clinics with years of misdirected effort behind them.

Basic cycle literacy is also missing.

16.7% of women did not know their own cycle length. Among women who described their cycles as regular, 10% still could not state their cycle length. And 1.5% of the cohort reported their cycle and period as the same length. That is a fundamental misunderstanding of menstrual physiology that no app will fix.

Longer time trying correlates with lower knowledge.

Women trying for over 12 months showed lower fertile window knowledge (53.7%) than those trying under 12 months (62.6%). The longer you struggle without structured guidance, the less confident you become. That is not a personal failing. It is what happens when the healthcare system tells couples to "just relax and try for a year" without teaching them anything about their biology.

What charting changes.

In RRM, Creighton Model charting closes this gap from the first appointment. The chart makes the fertile window visible in real time. It removes the guesswork. It creates a data record that informs every clinical decision that follows. But it does more than that. It reveals what the cycle is actually telling you: ovulation timing, luteal phase adequacy, mucus quality, cycle length variation. Irregular cycles are not just inconvenient. They are a clinical signal that prompts evaluation for PCOS, thyroid dysfunction, luteal phase defect, and other treatable conditions.

The appropriate response to cycle confusion is not a better app. It is a clinical workup with a provider who reads your chart.

A note on the study.

This research was funded by Hertility Health Ltd, a UK company that sells digital fertility assessments. Their business model benefits from demonstrating knowledge gaps. That does not invalidate the findings (the sample size is enormous and the direction aligns with other literature), but it is worth knowing. The study also collected no data from male partners, despite conception being a couple process.

What this means for you

  • If you are charting, you are already ahead of 41% of women trying to conceive. That is not a small thing.
  • If your cycles are irregular, do not accept "that's just how your body is." Irregular cycles are a clinical finding, not a personality trait. Ask for evaluation.
  • If you have been trying for more than 12 months and no one has taught you to identify your fertile window with precision, that is a gap in your care, not a gap in your effort.
  • Ask your provider: can you read my chart? If they cannot, that tells you something about the care model.

The Surgery That Found What Everyone Else Missed

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

Patient Angle

Case D was told to get a hysterectomy. Instead, she got an NCL with S-MAP protocol. They found cancer no one else had seen, treated it, and she brought home a baby. That is the difference between a surgery that removes organs and one that investigates them. When 89.1% of laparoscopies previously called "normal" reveal missed endometriosis under near-contact lens magnification, the question is not whether the disease was there. The question is whether anyone looked hard enough to find it.

Key Evidence

  • 89.1% of prior "normal" laparoscopies had missed endometriosis when re-examined with NCL technique (Hilgers Ch. 63)
  • 4 occult cancers found incidentally during systematic mapping -- probability of finding this many by chance: 0.0000000001
  • S-MAP (Systematic Mapping and Pathology) protocol ensures every peritoneal surface is examined and biopsied, not just visually obvious lesions
  • Case D: recommended for hysterectomy elsewhere, NCL/S-MAP revealed treatable pathology including malignancy, preserved fertility, achieved live birth

The PCOS Treatment Your Doctor Probably Has Not Mentioned

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

Patient Angle

You were told you have PCOS. You were handed metformin or birth control and told to come back when you want to get pregnant. But nobody mentioned that ovarian wedge resection -- a surgical approach refined over 90 years -- achieves 91% ovulation and 92% pregnancy rates. Nobody mentioned that myo-inositol, a naturally occurring compound, restores ovulation in 86% of cases in randomized trials. These are not fringe treatments. They are published, replicated, and available. The problem is not that the evidence does not exist. The problem is that no one brought it to your appointment.

Key Evidence

  • OWR (Ovarian Wedge Resection): 91% ovulation rate, 92% pregnancy rate (Halbe 1972 series, via Hilgers Ch. 75)
  • Myo-inositol: 86% ovulation restoration in clomiphene-resistant PCOS (Nestler, NEJM, 1999)
  • Meta-analysis (Greff 2023): Inositol confirmed non-inferior to metformin for insulin resistance, testosterone, cycle normalization, with superior tolerability
  • Historical lineage: Stein-Leventhal 1935 first described the condition; Stein 1965 long-term OWR follow-up; Adashi 1981 confirmed mechanism
  • Adhesion concern addressed: Modern microsurgical technique and letrozole addendum reduce historical adhesion risks

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

He got a semen analysis. It came back "normal" or "slightly low." Nobody did a physical exam. Nobody checked for varicocele. Nobody ran hormones. Nobody assessed DNA fragmentation. Then someone recommended IVF with ICSI -- a procedure that bypasses the male problem entirely instead of diagnosing and treating it. In 1,014 couples who actually got a complete andrological workup, "idiopathic" male infertility dropped from the standard 30-50% to just 8%. And 40.9% conceived naturally with targeted treatment. If nobody looked past his semen analysis, the evaluation is not complete.

After Five Losses and No Answers, One Hormone Test Changed Everything

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

Five pregnancies. Five losses. The standard RPL workup found nothing: no clotting disorder, no anatomical anomaly, no chromosomal abnormality. She was told it was "bad luck" and to "try again." What nobody tested was her androgen levels. RRM evaluation found hypoandrogenemia -- an insufficient level of DHEA-S that compromises endometrial receptivity and early pregnancy support. It is not on the standard RPL panel. When it was identified and treated, the outcome changed. This is not one case. Hilgers Chapter 57 reports a 79% subsequent live birth rate in women with three or more prior losses. Chapter 50 reports 84% for women 37 and under. These are not women with easy histories. These are the ones conventional medicine could not help.

A 2.5% Repeat Surgery Rate. That Is What Excision Looks Like.

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

Patient Angle

You had surgery for endometriosis. The pain came back. You had another surgery. The pain came back again. You are starting to think the disease is unbeatable. It is not unbeatable. It was not properly removed. Cauterization and ablation destroy the surface of endometriosis lesions while leaving the root intact. Recurrence with these techniques hits 98.7% in long-term follow-up. Excision -- cutting the lesion out completely, root and all -- drops the repeat surgery rate to 2.5% in a 620-patient series. That is not a marginal improvement. That is a fundamentally different outcome. The disease is the same. The surgery is not.

Key Evidence

  • 2.5% repeat surgery rate with complete excision (Yeung et al. 2024, n=620)
  • 98.7% recurrence rate with cauterization/ablation in long-term follow-up (Hilgers Ch. 79)
  • Excision site recurrence: 7.2% vs vaporization 22.3% -- three-fold reduction at the lesion level
  • Teenager data: Yeung 2011 confirmed excision is safe and effective in adolescents, relevant for early intervention
  • Deep infiltrating disease: Yeung 2016 DRP (deeply infiltrating rectovaginal endo) data shows excision feasibility even in advanced disease

Your Pregnancy May Be Decided Before the Embryo Arrives

Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

Patient Angle

You were told your progesterone was "fine." They drew it on day 21. But your cycle is 32 days, not 28, which means day 21 was not even close to your mid-luteal peak. That single blood draw -- timed to an assumption about your body instead of to your actual ovulation -- may have missed the problem entirely. Seventy-five percent of early pregnancy losses are now attributed to implantation failure, not embryonic defects. And 55% of healthy, ovulating women had at least one short luteal phase over a 12-month observation period. The luteal phase is not a background process. It is the foundation the embryo lands on. If nobody measured it correctly, nobody knows whether it was adequate.

Key Evidence

  • 75% of early pregnancy losses attributed to implantation failure, not embryonic defects (Ng et al. 2020)
  • 55% of healthy ovulating women had at least one short luteal phase over 12 months (Henry/Prior 2024)
  • Day-21 progesterone is fundamentally flawed: Ch. 19 case study demonstrates 92.7%/96.3% targeting accuracy only when timed to actual ovulation via charting, not calendar assumption
  • Prior's body of work (1982-2025): Decades of evidence that ovulatory disturbances are common, subclinical, and clinically consequential -- not rare edge cases
