# RRM Academy Audience Personas

> Who we serve, how they think, where they are in their journey, and what moves them to act.

**Last updated:** 2026-03-01

---

## Segment Map

RRM Academy serves two primary segments with distinct needs, decision processes, and content paths. Within each segment, personas represent different entry points and motivations.

```
Patients/Couples (80% of traffic)
├── The Searcher (early awareness)
├── The Exhausted Patient (failed conventional care)
├── The Values-Driven Couple (proactive choice)
└── The Cost-Conscious Couple (practical constraint)

Healthcare Professionals (20% of traffic)
├── The Curious Generalist (exploring RRM)
├── The Frustrated OBGYN (dissatisfied with suppressive protocols)
└── The FABM Professional (expanding scope)
```

## Patient Personas

### Persona 1: The Searcher

**"I just Googled my symptoms and I need to understand what's wrong with me."**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Demographics | Woman, 24-38, college-educated, likely in a relationship |
| Stage | Early awareness -- has symptoms but no clear diagnosis or is newly diagnosed |
| Trigger | Painful periods, irregular cycles, difficulty conceiving, or a friend's recommendation |
| Search behavior | "why are my periods so painful," "PCOS treatment without birth control," "endometriosis symptoms," "what is RRM" |
| Emotional state | Confused, overwhelmed, mildly anxious. Doesn't know what she doesn't know. |
| Current care | Primary care or OBGYN who prescribed hormonal contraception for symptom management |

**Mental models at play:**
- **Mere exposure effect:** She's never heard of RRM. The first encounter needs to build familiarity, not close a sale.
- **Status quo bias:** She's been told birth control is the answer. Switching paradigms requires trust.
- **Availability heuristic:** IVF is the only fertility treatment she can name because it's culturally visible. RRM doesn't have that mindshare yet.
- **Confirmation bias (opportunity):** She already suspects something is wrong with the "just take the pill" advice. Content that validates her instinct builds immediate trust.

**What she needs from RRM Academy:**
1. Plain-language explanation of what's happening in her body
2. Validation that her symptoms are real and treatable, not "normal"
3. A clear next step that doesn't feel like a sales pitch
4. Evidence that this approach works (not just testimonials -- citations)

**Content path:**
`what-is-rrm pillar` → specific condition page (endometriosis, PCOS) → course preview → free course (RRM vs IVF or Postpartum) → email list → paid course

**Key objection:** "Is this real medicine or alternative/natural wellness stuff?"
**Counter:** Clinical tone, MD authorship, peer-reviewed citations, university affiliations. The site's e-ink aesthetic communicates "serious" without trying.

**Messaging that moves her:**
- "Have you ever been told your painful periods are 'normal'?"
- "You deserve a diagnosis, not a prescription."
- "Evidence-based. Root-cause. Couple-centered."

### Persona 2: The Exhausted Patient

**"I've done three rounds of Clomid, two IUIs, and my doctor is now recommending IVF. There has to be another way."**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Demographics | Woman or couple, 30-42, middle to upper-middle income |
| Stage | Deep consideration -- has tried conventional treatments, possibly including failed IVF |
| Trigger | Treatment fatigue, financial strain, physical toll of hormonal stimulation, or a friend/forum mention of RRM |
| Search behavior | "IVF alternative," "RRM vs IVF," "NaProTechnology near me," "fertility treatment that treats the cause," "why does IVF keep failing" |
| Emotional state | Frustrated, exhausted, possibly grieving. Trust in the medical system is damaged. Skeptical but desperately hopeful. |
| Current care | REI who has recommended IVF or more IVF cycles |
| Spending | Has already spent $10K-$50K+ on fertility treatments |

**Mental models at play:**
- **Sunk cost fallacy:** She's invested so much in the conventional path that switching feels like admitting failure. Content must reframe switching as progress, not retreat.
- **Loss aversion:** "What if I waste more time on RRM and my fertility window closes?" This is the REI's most effective weapon. Must be addressed directly with timeline data.
- **Endowment effect (negative):** She "owns" her diagnosis of unexplained infertility. Learning that her condition was diagnosable all along triggers anger (at prior providers) and relief simultaneously.
- **Regret aversion:** Afraid of regretting either choice. RRM Academy must reduce perceived risk (cost comparison, evidence, "this isn't instead of, this is finding out why").

**What she needs from RRM Academy:**
1. Evidence that RRM works for women like her (outcome data by age, by diagnosis, after failed IVF)
2. Honest comparison of timelines (RRM workup vs. IVF timeline)
3. Cost transparency
4. A provider she can actually see

**Content path:**
`"RRM vs IVF" search` → what-is-rrm comparison section → Boyle 2025 charts → free RRM vs IVF course → find a provider → paid endometriosis masterclass

**Key objection:** "I don't have time to try something new. My eggs are aging."
**Counter:** Boyle 2025 average time to conception: 12 months. IVF average 2-3 cycles over 9-18 months. RRM diagnostic workup: 2-3 months. The delay differential is months, not years. And RRM treats conditions that cause IVF to fail repeatedly. (Address directly in content; never dismiss the concern.)

**Messaging that moves her:**
- "41% live birth rate. No embryos created. No multiple pregnancies."
- "If three rounds of Clomid haven't worked, there's a reason. We find it."
- "The average couple spends $40,000-$60,000 on IVF. RRM costs a fraction of that."

### Persona 3: The Values-Driven Couple

**"We want to conceive without IVF, and not just for religious reasons."**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Demographics | Couple (both partners engaged), 26-40, may be Catholic/religious but not necessarily |
| Stage | Decided -- has already decided against IVF, now looking for the best non-ART path |
| Trigger | Ethical concerns about embryo disposition, desire for in vivo conception, philosophical commitment to treating the body rather than bypassing it |
| Search behavior | "NaProTechnology," "ethical fertility treatment," "fertility treatment without IVF," "Catholic infertility treatment" |
| Emotional state | Resolved but underserved. Feels dismissed by mainstream fertility medicine. Grateful when they find providers who share their values. |
| Current care | May be working with a FABM instructor already, possibly seeking or already under RRM physician care |

**Mental models at play:**
- **Unity principle:** They respond strongly to "one of us" signals. RRM Academy's couple-centered language and values alignment create instant trust.
- **Commitment & consistency:** They've already committed to a values position. Content that affirms this commitment deepens engagement.
- **Mimetic desire:** Seeing other couples like them succeed reinforces their choice.

**What they need from RRM Academy:**
1. Confirmation that evidence supports their choice (they're not sacrificing outcomes for values)
2. Practical guidance: how to find a provider, what to expect, timelines
3. Community connection (STUC membership)
4. Deep educational content (not just reassurance)

**Content path:**
`NaProTechnology search` → what-is-rrm NaPro section → condition-specific courses → STUC community → provider directory → advanced courses

**Key objection:** "Will people think we're anti-science?"
**Counter:** Boyle 2025 and Sanchez-Mendez 2025 data. 62.1% adjusted cumulative live birth rate is not anti-science. Framing: "Your values and the evidence point the same direction."

**Messaging that moves them:**
- "62.1% adjusted cumulative take-home baby rate at 36 months."
- "In vivo conception. Treated cause. Your body, restored."
- "Couples, not patients."

**Important note:** This persona is the one adversaries most want to define RRM by. Do not center messaging on this persona alone or the religious angle. The site correctly leads with evidence and treats values-driven couples as one segment among many, not the defining audience.

### Persona 4: The Cost-Conscious Couple

**"We can't afford IVF. We literally cannot spend $30,000."**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Demographics | Couple, 25-40, working class to middle class, often uninsured or underinsured for fertility |
| Stage | Constrained awareness -- knows about IVF, can't access it, may not know alternatives exist |
| Trigger | Insurance denial for IVF, sticker shock at initial IVF consultation, or research into affordable options |
| Search behavior | "affordable fertility treatment," "fertility treatment cost," "infertility help without IVF," "how much does NaProTechnology cost" |
| Emotional state | Anxious about money and time. Feels excluded from fertility care by economics. May feel shame about not being able to afford what seems like the "normal" path. |
| Current care | OBGYN only (can't afford REI). May have Medicaid or high-deductible plan. |

**Mental models at play:**
- **Zero-price effect:** Free courses (RRM vs IVF, Postpartum) have disproportionate appeal.
- **Mental accounting:** "$150/month for a course" feels different than "$1,800/year." Frame favorably.
- **Anchoring:** The $40K-$60K IVF anchor makes RRM costs feel trivial by comparison.
- **Contrast effect:** RRM's diagnostic approach uses tests and treatments already covered by standard insurance. This contrast with IVF's cash-pay model is powerful.

**What they need from RRM Academy:**
1. Clear cost information (what does RRM actually cost?)
2. Insurance navigation (what's covered, what isn't)
3. Free educational content to start
4. A path to care that doesn't require a $5K deposit

**Content path:**
`"affordable fertility treatment" search` → what-is-rrm cost section → free courses → email list → provider directory → STUC community

**Key objection:** "If it's cheaper, it's probably worse."
**Counter:** Cost section on pillar page showing why RRM costs less (diagnostic workup, not procedures; standard labs, not proprietary technology; shorter treatment timelines for many conditions).

**Messaging that moves them:**
- "Most RRM diagnostic tests are covered by standard health insurance."
- "Average IVF: $40,000-$60,000. RRM: a fraction of that."
- "Start with our free courses. No credit card required."

## Healthcare Professional Personas

### Persona 5: The Curious Generalist

**"I keep hearing about RRM from patients. What actually is it?"**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Role | OBGYN, family medicine, or internal medicine physician |
| Age | 30-55 |
| Training | Completed residency 5-25 years ago. No fellowship in REI. |
| Current practice | Office-based gynecology, may prescribe Clomid/letrozole but refers complex infertility to REI |
| Knowledge of RRM | Minimal. May confuse it with "natural family planning." |

**Mental models at play:**
- **Circle of competence:** They know they don't know enough about fertility. RRM Academy is a safe way to expand competence without a fellowship.
- **Authority bias:** They'll trust content authored by a board-certified OBGYN (Dr. Naomi) more than content from a patient advocacy site.
- **Foot-in-the-door:** A single CME module or free course lowers the barrier. Once they've invested time, they're more likely to continue.

**What they need:**
1. CME-eligible education (makes the time investment professionally valuable)
2. Clinical protocols they can implement in their existing practice
3. Evidence they can cite when colleagues question RRM
4. Clarity on what's within their scope vs. what needs fellowship training

**Content path:**
`what-is-rrm provider callouts` → training section → free introductory course → CME courses → STUC professional membership → conference/fellowship referral

**Key objection:** "I don't want to get labeled as anti-IVF or religious."
**Counter:** RRM is evidence-based reproductive medicine. Framing for professionals: "You already do most of this. RRM gives it a systematic diagnostic framework."

### Persona 6: The Frustrated OBGYN

**"I'm tired of prescribing birth control for everything. There has to be a better way."**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Role | OBGYN, 5-15 years in practice |
| Current frustration | Prescribing OCPs for endometriosis, PCOS, irregular cycles, and heavy bleeding because it's all they were trained to do |
| Interest in RRM | Active -- has already read about it, may have attended a conference |
| Barrier | Doesn't know where to start with formal training, concerned about peer perception |

**Mental models at play:**
- **Cognitive dissonance:** Knows current practice is suppressive, not curative. Actively seeking resolution.
- **Mimetic desire:** Seeing Dr. Naomi's practice model (UPMC appointment, academic credentials, published research) makes the RRM path feel professionally viable.
- **Commitment & consistency:** Once they take a course, they begin identifying as an RRM-curious provider. This identity shift drives further engagement.

**What they need:**
1. A credentialed, structured training pathway
2. Professional community (they'll feel isolated in their department)
3. Patient outcomes data they can reference
4. Practice integration guidance (how to add RRM to an existing OBGYN practice)

**Content path:**
Direct to training page → CME courses → STUC professional membership → IIRRM fellowship referral → ongoing CME

**Messaging that moves them:**
- "The training on hormones is abysmal and antiquated in medical school and even OBGYN residency."
- "You became a doctor to heal, not to suppress."
- "CME-eligible. Evidence-based. Integrates into your existing practice."

### Persona 7: The FABM Professional

**"I teach Creighton/Marquette/STM and my clients keep asking about medical follow-up."**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Role | Fertility awareness educator, FertilityCare Practitioner, FABM instructor |
| Training | Certified in one or more FABMs, not a physician |
| Current scope | Teaches charting, identifies abnormalities, refers to physicians for treatment |
| Interest in RRM | High -- RRM is the medical arm that completes what they started |

**Mental models at play:**
- **Jobs to be done:** They're hired to teach charting, but their clients need treatment. RRM Academy courses help them understand what the physician does with the chart data.
- **Network effects:** Every FABM professional who recommends RRM Academy creates a referral channel.

**What they need:**
1. Understanding of RRM diagnostics and treatment (not to practice, but to explain to clients)
2. Confidence to refer clients to RRM providers
3. Professional development in the medical context of their work

**Content path:**
`FABM section of what-is-rrm` → free courses → condition-specific courses → STUC community → provider directory (for referrals)

## Psychographic Overlays

These traits cut across personas:

### Information Appetite

| Level | Personas | Content preference |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| Scanner | The Searcher, Cost-Conscious | TL;DR boxes, bullet points, stat cards, infographics |
| Reader | Exhausted Patient, Values-Driven | Long-form pillar content, comparison tables, citations |
| Researcher | Skeptical Academic (adversary), Curious Generalist | Research library, full-text articles, methodology sections |
| Practitioner | Frustrated OBGYN, FABM Professional | Protocols, case studies, CME modules |

### Trust Signals (Ranked by Impact)

| Signal | Why it works | Mental model |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| MD authorship | Credentials > claims | Authority bias |
| Peer-reviewed citations | "This isn't opinion" | Authority bias + confirmation bias |
| Outcome data with charts | Visual evidence is more convincing than prose | Availability heuristic |
| University affiliation (UPMC) | Institutional backing | Authority bias |
| Nonprofit status (501c3) | "They're not selling me something" | Reciprocity + trust |
| Free courses | Generous = trustworthy | Reciprocity principle |
| Research library (3,000+ articles) | Depth signals authority | Mere exposure + authority |
| Patient/couple language | "They understand me" | Liking / similarity bias |

### Emotional Journey Map

```
Awareness: Confusion → Validation ("I'm not crazy")
            ↓
Consideration: Fear → Confidence ("The evidence supports this")
            ↓
Decision: Hesitation → Commitment ("I'm doing this")
            ↓
Advocacy: Gratitude → Evangelism ("Everyone needs to know about this")
```

The site's current tone correctly matches the middle stages (confidence, commitment). The what-is-rrm pillar page handles awareness-to-consideration. Course content handles decision. STUC community handles advocacy.

## How Adversary Personas Interact with Audience Personas

| Adversary tactic | Which audience it targets | How to neutralize |
|-----------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| "RRM is anti-abortion ideology" | The Searcher, Cost-Conscious | International evidence (Madrid, Dublin). Clinical tone. Never mention politics. |
| "RRM delays treatment" | The Exhausted Patient | Timeline comparison data. Boyle 2025: 12 months avg. |
| "RRM has no evidence" | Curious Generalist, Exhausted Patient | Peer-reviewed citations, outcome charts, research library |
| "RRM is just basic OBGYN" | Frustrated OBGYN | If true, why isn't it being practiced? The training gap is the point. |
| Clock anxiety pressure | Exhausted Patient, Cost-Conscious | Age-stratified data (Sanchez-Mendez: 83.7% for age 18-30, 53.3% for 36-40). Honest about over-40 limitations. |

## Gaps and Next Steps

4. **Professional onboarding sequence** -- email nurture for provider personas who take a free course.
5. **International evidence emphasis** -- Madrid, Dublin, Australia data should be more prominent to counter the "American culture war" frame.
