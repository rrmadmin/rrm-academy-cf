# Claim Audit: /art-registries-and-codes/

**Date:** 2026-05-12
**Page:** https://rrmacademy.org/art-registries-and-codes/
**Purpose:** Catalog every assertion a pro-IVF reader could challenge as a "claim," so each can be tied to a primary-source citation.

**Legend:**
- `[C]` Already cited inline (links to library record or named author/year/journal)
- `[U]` Uncited — needs primary-source citation
- `[D]` Definitional / stipulative — true by definition, but a hostile reader may still demand backing
- `[S]` Soft (qualitative) — true but unverifiable without the framework; consider softening or removing

---

## Section 1: Page-level framing

| # | Claim | Status | Notes |
|---|-------|--------|-------|
| 1.1 | "ART registries and codes of practice are the two accountability instruments that govern assisted reproductive technology worldwide." | [S] | Universal framing claim. A pro-IVF reader could argue there are other accountability instruments (clinical guidelines, malpractice law, insurer audits). Consider "two of the principal accountability instruments." |
| 1.2 | "A code of practice is the rulebook a fertility clinic must follow to operate legally in a given jurisdiction." | [D] | Definitional. Defensible. |
| 1.3 | "A registry is the dataset of cycle outcomes those clinics are required to submit." | [D] | Definitional but loses precision: voluntary registries (DIR, CARTR Plus, REDLARA) are not "required." Soften to "submit, often as a condition of licence, accreditation, or society membership." |

## Summary: high-priority citation gaps for a pro-IVF reader to attack

Ordered by aggressiveness a critical reader would apply:

1. **"The registries' real structural function is to serve the industry that populates them" (22.2).** Central thesis claim. No citation. **Mandatory cite:** Kushnir 2017, Gleicher 2019, Wilkinson 2017, or similar peer-reviewed registry-governance critique.
2. **"Society-run registries operate without external audit, governed by the same societies whose member clinics fund them" (23.2, restated 25.1).** Specific governance-conflict claim. **Mandatory cite:** ASRM/SART board composition documents, financial disclosures, or a published conflict-of-interest analysis.
3. **"Apparent per-cycle live birth rates can diverge between countries by 10 percentage points or more" (4.1).** Quantitative comparative claim. **Mandatory cite:** ESHRE EIM cross-country variation table or a meta-analysis of cross-country variability.

## Patterns observed (for future citation density work)

- **Universal-negative claims are the most attackable.** Every "registries do not...", "no major registry publishes...", "is not tracked by any..." statement is a target. Either soften (state which registries) or cite the comparison-frame data as the source.
- **Superlative claims are the second-most attackable.** "One of the most...", "among the first...", "the only..." — each needs a comparison or a defined ranking source.
- **Multi-jurisdiction policy claims need per-jurisdiction citation.** The Canadian provincial + Latin American country funding claims are each lists of three or more uncited assertions.
- **Quantitative ranges need explicit anchoring.** "1 to 5 percent OHSS," "$12,000 to $15,000 per cycle," "10 percentage points divergence" — every range needs a source.
- **Editorial framing claims (22.2, 23.2, 25.1) form the page's central argument and are the most rhetorically exposed.** These should carry the highest-quality citations on the page, not the least.

## Anti-patterns to avoid in remediation

- Do not cite the comparison-frame cards as the source for prose claims downstream — the cards' own claims are themselves under audit.
- Do not chain "according to RRM Academy's analysis" as a fallback — that's self-citation.
- Do not invent PMIDs or DOIs for citations the library does not already hold. Run each through `rrm-cli search` or Perplexity verification before insertion (per the citation-integrity rule in CLAUDE.md).
