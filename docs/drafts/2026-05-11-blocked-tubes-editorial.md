# ARTICLE

**Title:** Your HSG Says Your Tubes Are Blocked. Your Doctor Says IVF. Here Is What You Were Not Told.

**Byline:** Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

**Meta description (154 chars):** Blocked fallopian tubes on HSG do not mean IVF is your only option. Learn about three restorative procedures, FTR, microsurgical anastomosis, and neosalpingostomy, and why 60% of HSG "blockages" are not real occlusions at all.

---

You sat in an exam room. The radiologist called it. Bilateral proximal occlusion. Both tubes blocked. Your OB's office called the next day: "We're going to refer you to a reproductive endocrinologist."

You did not ask what that meant. You already knew. IVF. Tens of thousands of dollars. Injections, retrieval, waiting, maybe an embryo that does not make it. And the unspoken message underneath all of it: your body cannot do this on its own.

That message is incomplete. In many cases, it is wrong.

Here is what the referral letter did not include.

---

## The HSG Is a Screening Test, Not a Final Answer

A hysterosalpingogram is an X-ray procedure that pushes contrast dye through the uterus and tubes. When dye does not pass, the report says "blockage." It is a reasonable first-look test. It is not a diagnosis.

A major professional society committee opinion published in *Fertility and Sterility* (2015) reported that in 60% of patients where HSG showed proximal tubal blockage, a repeat HSG one month later showed patent tubes. Sixty percent. Same patient. Same tubes. Different result.

The explanation is physiological. The proximal tube, the narrow segment where the tube meets the uterus, is prone to temporary obstruction. A 2023 open-access review in *CVIR Endovascular* (Roberts, 2023) found that among women with proximal occlusion on HSG, approximately 40% had mucus plugs or debris in the tube, and another 20% had uterotubal spasm: the tube contracted during the procedure itself. Those are not structural blockages. They are functional events. Often reversible without surgery.

The remaining minority of proximal "blockages" involves true organic occlusion from scarring, endometriosis, salpingitis isthmica nodosa, or chronic infection. Those cases warrant treatment. But the treatment is not IVF.

IVF does not treat the tube. It bypasses it. The blockage remains. The disease causing it, if there is one, remains. The tube is simply removed from the equation by moving fertilization into a lab.

Restorative reproductive medicine asks a different question: what is actually wrong with this tube, and can we fix it?

---

## Tubal Factor: One of the Most Common Causes of Female Infertility

Tubal disease accounts for roughly 25 to 35% of female infertility, based on pooled data reviewed in a *Fertility and Sterility* meta-analysis (Honoré et al., 1999). It is one of the leading structural causes clinicians encounter, and it is not automatically a dead end.

The standard pathway, HSG finding routes to IVF referral, skips an entire tier of options that are evidence-based, minimally invasive, and far less expensive. Those options depend on where in the tube the problem actually sits.

---

## Three Pathways, Based on Where the Blockage Is

Tubal anatomy divides into three segments: proximal (near the uterus), mid-segment, and distal (the fimbriated end near the ovary). The cause of blockage and the appropriate restorative approach differ by segment. RRM clinicians work through this systematically.

---

### Pathway 1: Proximal Blockage

**Fallopian tube recanalization (FTR)**

This is the most common site of "blockage" found on screening HSG, and the most frequently mismanaged.

Fallopian tube recanalization is a minimally invasive outpatient procedure performed under fluoroscopic guidance. A thin catheter is threaded through the cervix and uterus to the tubal opening. A guidewire clears debris or gently displaces the obstruction. The procedure typically takes under 30 minutes. No general anesthesia. No hospital stay.

Roberts (2023) reports technical patency rates of 70 to 100% across pooled literature. Intrauterine pregnancy rates following the procedure range from 20 to 60%, with an average around 30% across studies. A 2024 study in *Therapeutic Advances in Reproductive Health* (Sun et al., 2024) followed 498 women post-FTR and found that concurrent chronic endometritis, a treatable condition, significantly reduced post-procedure pregnancy rates. The implication: the procedure works better when underlying inflammatory conditions are identified and treated first. That is exactly what restorative care does.

The major professional society's own 2015 committee opinion states that [selective salpingography](/glossary/selective-salpingography) and transcervical tubal cannulation should be considered before proceeding to more invasive or costly interventions for proximal occlusion. The evidence has been in the literature for decades. The IVF referrals have been happening anyway.

Who is a candidate: women with proximal tubal occlusion on HSG, no evidence of active infection, no hydrosalpinx, no suspicion of genital tuberculosis. An RRM clinician evaluates imaging, cycle history, and both partners before recommending this approach.

---

### Pathway 2: Mid-Segment Blockage

**Tubo-tubal microsurgical anastomosis**

Mid-segment blockage most commonly follows prior tubal ligation, a previous ectopic pregnancy where the tube was partially resected, or endometriosis-related adhesions within the tube itself.

The restorative approach is microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis. A surgeon removes the blocked segment and reconnects the healthy ends under microscopic magnification. This is a more involved surgery than FTR, performed in the operating room, but it is restorative in the anatomic sense: the tube is reconstructed, not bypassed.

The Honoré et al. (1999) meta-analysis reported that "ongoing intrauterine pregnancy rates near 50% can be achieved in patients with proximal blockage" when cases are carefully selected. Restorative surgical centers report comparable figures for mid-segment anastomosis in well-selected patients, though outcomes depend on the length and quality of remaining tube and whether endometriosis is concurrently excised.

On endometriosis: it requires separate evaluation and surgical excision at the same operative setting, not hormonal suppression and deferral.

Who is a candidate: women seeking reversal of prior sterilization, those with post-ectopic tube loss and adequate remaining tubal length, or patients with mid-segment adhesive disease from endometriosis. The male partner's semen analysis informs candidacy as well.

---

### Pathway 3: Distal Blockage

**Neosalpingostomy or fimbrioplasty**

Distal blockage typically means the fimbriated end of the tube, the part that sweeps the egg at ovulation, has scarred closed. The most common cause is hydrosalpinx: a tube that has filled with fluid from past infection, often pelvic inflammatory disease or chlamydia, and become dilated and sealed at the end. Endometriosis affecting the distal tube or ovarian adhesions anchoring the tube away from the ovary also fall here.

Surgical options include neosalpingostomy (opening the sealed end and everting the tissue to create a new opening) and fimbrioplasty (reconstructing and freeing adherent fimbriae). Both are laparoscopic procedures. Outcomes are more variable than with proximal repair because distal disease reflects a larger burden of pelvic pathology, and the reconstructed tube may not recover full normal function.

For hydrosalpinx specifically, the RRM clinician must also consider the evidence that a fluid-filled tube can impair uterine receptivity if left untreated. That is not an argument for IVF. It is an argument for treating the tube.

Who is a candidate: women with documented hydrosalpinx, distal tubal adhesions from endometriosis or prior infection, or fimbrial occlusion without severe mid-tubal damage. The extent of tubal dilation and mucosal preservation factor heavily into surgical prognosis.

---

## What IVF Does Not Do

IVF retrieves eggs, fertilizes them in a laboratory, and transfers an embryo into the uterus. The fallopian tube plays no role. That is the point. When the tube is bypassed, the question of what is wrong with it never has to be answered.

For some couples, bypass is appropriate. For many, it is a shortcut that leaves the underlying condition unaddressed and the couple no closer to understanding what actually blocked the tube.

If the blockage was mucus or spasm, IVF treats a problem that may not exist.

If the blockage was endometriosis, IVF proceeds while active disease remains in the pelvis, affecting the uterine environment and implantation.

If the blockage was infection-related scarring, the conditions that caused it are still present.

RRM clinicians treat the tube. They treat what caused it to block. That sequence matters.

---

## What to Do If You Have Been Told IVF Is Your Only Option

It may not be. The path forward depends on what is actually causing the blockage, which requires evaluation by a clinician trained to investigate tubal anatomy and pelvic disease.

An RRM clinician will evaluate both partners. Male factor is solely responsible in roughly 20% of couples presenting with infertility and contributes in another 30 to 40%. A thorough evaluation does not start and end with one partner's tubes.

If you have received an HSG result showing blocked tubes and been offered IVF as the immediate next step, you have not yet seen the full clinical picture. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can blocked fallopian tubes be unblocked without IVF?**

Yes, in many cases. The right approach depends on where in the tube the blockage is located and what is causing it. Proximal blockage near the uterus is the most common finding on screening HSG. A significant proportion of those findings are mucus plugs, debris, or tubal spasm rather than true structural occlusion. Fallopian tube recanalization (FTR) is a minimally invasive outpatient procedure that restores patency in 70 to 100% of proximal occlusion cases in pooled literature (Roberts, 2023). Mid-segment blockage from prior ligation or ectopic pregnancy may be addressed with microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis. Distal blockage and hydrosalpinx can be treated with neosalpingostomy or fimbrioplasty. IVF bypasses the tube without treating it. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**2. What is selective HSG?**

[Selective salpingography](/glossary/selective-salpingography) is a targeted procedure that follows a diagnostic HSG showing tubal blockage. Where a diagnostic HSG introduces contrast broadly through the uterus, selective salpingography positions a catheter directly at the opening of the blocked tube to apply focused pressure and confirm whether the obstruction is structural or functional. It can both diagnose and treat proximal blockage in the same sitting by clearing debris or a mucus plug. Major professional society guidelines recommend selective salpingography before more invasive or costly approaches for proximal tubal occlusion. The procedure is outpatient, requires no general anesthesia, and is performed by a clinician with interventional training. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**3. How successful is fallopian tube recanalization?**

Technical success, meaning the tube is confirmed open at the end of the procedure, is reported at 70 to 100% in pooled literature (Roberts, 2023). Intrauterine pregnancy rates following FTR range from 20 to 60%, with an average around 30% across studies. Outcomes vary based on the underlying cause of blockage, the presence of other conditions such as endometriosis or chronic endometritis, age, and ovarian reserve. Women with secondary infertility and shorter duration of infertility tend to have better results. These are rates of natural conception following restoration of the tube. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**4. Are blocked tubes a sign I need IVF?**

No. A blocked-tube finding on HSG is a starting point for investigation. HSG has a documented false-positive rate for proximal blockage: major professional society data shows that 60% of patients with proximal occlusion on HSG have patent tubes on repeat testing one month later. When true blockage is confirmed, the location and cause determine the treatment. Restorative procedures exist for all three tubal segments and are evidence-based, minimally invasive, and far less expensive than IVF. IVF does not treat the tube. It circumvents it. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**5. Who treats blocked tubes with restorative procedures?**

[RRM clinicians](/what-is-rrm/) and restorative reproductive surgeons. [NaProTechnology](/naprotechnology/)-trained physicians are trained in the surgical and diagnostic approach to tubal factor infertility within a root-cause framework. This includes surgical evaluation and intervention, cycle-based hormonal assessment, and treatment of underlying conditions including endometriosis, pelvic adhesions, and chronic infection, all of which can contribute to tubal blockage. Interventional radiologists with fertility training also perform fluoroscopic FTR. Not all gynecologists or fertility specialists offer these procedures. The standard IVF-first pathway does not require tubal evaluation or repair. Ask specifically whether a clinician performs selective salpingography, tubo-tubal anastomosis, or neosalpingostomy before accepting a referral that routes directly to assisted reproduction. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.
