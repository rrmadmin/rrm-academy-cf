# ARTICLE

**Title tag (SERP):** Blocked Tubes on HSG: 3 Restorative Options Before IVF *(52 chars)*

**H1 (in-page):** Blocked Tubes Aren't Actually a Ticket to IVF... Over 60% Can Be Fixed.

**Byline:** Dr. Naomi Whittaker, MD, Board-Certified OBGYN, MIGS, NFPMC, FCI

**Meta description (148 chars):** Blocked fallopian tubes on HSG do not mean IVF. Three restorative procedures, FTR, anastomosis, and neosalpingostomy, can open most blocked tubes.

---

In restorative reproductive medicine, blocked fallopian tubes are addressed at the location of the blockage, with three procedures matched to where it sits in the tube. Fallopian tube recanalization addresses proximal blockage. Microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis reconnects mid-segment damage. Neosalpingostomy and fimbrioplasty open distal blockage and hydrosalpinx. This article walks through each and the evidence behind them.

---

## Do Blocked Tubes Have Symptoms?

Many women with blocked fallopian tubes have no symptoms at all and only learn of the diagnosis during a fertility workup. When symptoms do appear, they are most often pelvic pain, painful periods, or unusual vaginal discharge, the last is particularly associated with hydrosalpinx, where a fluid-filled tube is the source. There is no reliable home test. The diagnostic gateway is almost always the same: a hysterosalpingogram, or [HSG](/glossary/hsg/).

---

## The HSG Is a Screening Test, Not a Final Answer

A hysterosalpingogram is an X-ray procedure that pushes contrast dye through the uterus and tubes. When dye does not pass, the report says "blockage." It is a reasonable first-look test. It is not a diagnosis.

A 2015 practice committee opinion in *Fertility and Sterility* reported that in 60% of patients where HSG showed proximal tubal blockage, a repeat HSG one month later showed patent tubes. Sixty percent. Same patient. Same tubes. Different result.

The explanation is physiological. The proximal tube, the narrow segment where the tube meets the uterus, is prone to temporary obstruction. A 2023 open-access review in *CVIR Endovascular* (Roberts, 2023¹) found that among women with proximal occlusion on HSG, approximately 40% had mucus plugs or debris in the tube, and another 20% had uterotubal spasm: the tube contracted during the procedure itself. Those are not structural blockages. They are functional events. Often reversible without surgery.

The remaining minority of proximal "blockages" involves true organic occlusion from scarring, endometriosis, salpingitis isthmica nodosa, or chronic infection. Those cases warrant treatment. And treatment options exist.

Restorative reproductive medicine asks a different question: what is actually wrong with this tube, and can we fix it?

---

## Tubal Factor: One of the Most Common Causes of Female Infertility

[Tubal factor](/glossary/tubal-factor-infertility/) disease accounts for roughly 25 to 35% of female infertility, based on pooled data reviewed in a *Fertility and Sterility* meta-analysis (Honoré et al., 1999²). It is one of the leading structural causes clinicians encounter, and it is not automatically a dead end.

Evidence-based, minimally invasive, and far less expensive than IVF: restorative options exist for all three tubal segments. Which procedure is appropriate depends on where in the tube the problem actually sits.

## Three Pathways, Based on Where the Blockage Is

Tubal anatomy divides into three segments: proximal (near the uterus), mid-segment, and distal (the fimbriated end near the ovary). The cause of blockage and the appropriate restorative approach differ by segment. RRM clinicians work through this systematically.

---

### Pathway 1: Proximal Blockage

**Fallopian tube recanalization (FTR)**

This is the most common site of "blockage" found on screening HSG, and the one with the strongest restorative pathway.

Fallopian tube recanalization is a minimally invasive outpatient procedure performed under fluoroscopic guidance. A thin catheter is threaded through the cervix and uterus to the tubal opening. A guidewire clears debris or gently displaces the obstruction. The procedure typically takes under 30 minutes. No general anesthesia. No hospital stay.

Roberts (2023¹) reports technical patency rates of 70 to 100% across pooled literature. Intrauterine pregnancy rates following the procedure range from 20 to 60%, with an average around 30% across studies.

Across pooled outcomes, fallopian tube recanalization restores proximal patency in 70 to 100% of cases and yields intrauterine pregnancy in approximately one in three women, with no general anesthesia and a same-day discharge.

A 2024 study in *Therapeutic Advances in Reproductive Health* (Sun et al., 2024³) followed 498 women post-FTR and found that concurrent chronic endometritis, a treatable condition, significantly reduced post-procedure pregnancy rates. The implication: the procedure works better when underlying inflammatory conditions are identified and treated first. That is exactly what restorative care does.

The 2015 practice committee opinion states that [selective salpingography](/glossary/selective-salpingography/) and transcervical tubal cannulation should be considered before proceeding to more invasive or costly interventions for proximal occlusion.

Who is a candidate: women with proximal tubal occlusion on HSG, no evidence of active infection, no hydrosalpinx, no suspicion of genital tuberculosis. An RRM clinician evaluates imaging, cycle history, and both partners before recommending this approach.

---

### Pathway 2: Mid-Segment Blockage

**Tubo-tubal microsurgical anastomosis**

Mid-segment blockage most commonly follows prior [tubal ligation](/glossary/tubal-ligation-reversal/), a previous ectopic pregnancy where the tube was partially resected, or endometriosis-related adhesions within the tube itself.

The restorative approach is microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis. A surgeon removes the blocked segment and reconnects the healthy ends under microscopic magnification. This is a more involved surgery than FTR, performed in the operating room, but it is restorative in the anatomic sense: the tube is reconstructed, not bypassed.

The Honoré et al. (1999²) meta-analysis reported that "ongoing intrauterine pregnancy rates near 50% can be achieved in patients with proximal blockage" when cases are carefully selected. Restorative surgical centers report comparable figures for mid-segment anastomosis in well-selected patients, though outcomes depend on the length and quality of remaining tube and whether endometriosis is concurrently excised.

On endometriosis: it requires separate evaluation and surgical excision at the same operative setting, not hormonal suppression and deferral.

Who is a candidate: women seeking reversal of prior sterilization, those with post-ectopic tube loss and adequate remaining tubal length, or patients with mid-segment adhesive disease from endometriosis. The male partner's semen analysis informs candidacy as well.

---

### Pathway 3: Distal Blockage

**Neosalpingostomy or fimbrioplasty**

Distal blockage typically means the fimbriated end of the tube, the part that sweeps the egg at ovulation, has scarred closed. The most common cause is [hydrosalpinx](/glossary/hydrosalpinx/): a tube that has filled with fluid from past infection, often pelvic inflammatory disease or chlamydia, and become dilated and sealed at the end. Endometriosis affecting the distal tube or ovarian adhesions anchoring the tube away from the ovary also fall here.

Surgical options include [neosalpingostomy](/glossary/neosalpingostomy/) (opening the sealed end and everting the tissue to create a new opening) and fimbrioplasty (reconstructing and freeing adherent fimbriae). Both are laparoscopic procedures. Outcomes are more variable than with proximal repair because distal disease reflects a larger burden of pelvic pathology, and the reconstructed tube may not recover full normal function.

For hydrosalpinx specifically, the RRM clinician must also consider the evidence that a fluid-filled tube can impair uterine receptivity if left untreated. That is an argument for treating the tube directly.

Who is a candidate: women with documented hydrosalpinx, distal tubal adhesions from endometriosis or prior infection, or fimbrial occlusion without severe mid-tubal damage. The extent of tubal dilation and mucosal preservation factor heavily into surgical prognosis.

---

## How These Approaches Differ

IVF retrieves eggs, fertilizes them in a laboratory, and transfers an embryo into the uterus. The fallopian tube plays no role in that sequence. For couples where the tube cannot be restored, that may be the appropriate path.

Restorative reproductive medicine takes a different approach. The tube is evaluated. The underlying cause is identified. The goal is to restore anatomy and function so conception can occur naturally.

The distinction matters clinically. If the blockage was mucus or spasm, restoration removes the obstruction. If the blockage was endometriosis, treating the tube includes excising the disease at the same surgery. If the blockage stemmed from infection-related scarring, addressing the residual inflammatory environment can improve outcomes after repair.

RRM clinicians treat the tube and what caused it to block. That sequence is the point.

---

## Tubal Repair: What Is Available and Where to Find It

That quote, "Tubal surgery is all but obsolete," is from a review in *Reviews in Obstetrics and Gynecology*, describing how surgical repair of the fallopian tubes changed after the rise of assisted reproductive technology.⁴ The 2021 ASRM Practice Committee opinion frames the role of tubal surgery as selective and specialized rather than routine, reserved for cases where the surgical pathway is more appropriate or aligned with patient preference.⁵

Restorative procedures are available. Not every clinic offers them in-house, which means seeking a clinician with specific training in selective salpingography, microsurgical anastomosis, or neosalpingostomy may require a referral to a restorative reproductive surgeon or an RRM clinician. Asking specifically whether a clinician performs these procedures is a reasonable first question.

## What to Do Next

An HSG result showing blocked tubes is a starting point. It tells you something is worth investigating. It does not tell you what is causing the blockage, whether the blockage is real, or which restorative options apply to your situation.

An RRM clinician will evaluate both partners. Male factor is a common and frequently overlooked contributor to infertility, which is why a complete evaluation never starts and ends with one partner's tubes.

If you have received an HSG result showing blocked tubes, a clinician trained in tubal anatomy and pelvic disease can review your imaging, cycle history, and complete pelvic evaluation before you commit to any path. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can blocked fallopian tubes be unblocked without IVF?**

In restorative reproductive medicine, blocked fallopian tubes can often be treated without IVF, especially when the blockage is proximal. The right approach depends on where in the tube the blockage is located and what is causing it. Proximal blockage near the uterus is the most common finding on screening HSG, and a significant proportion of those findings are mucus plugs, debris, or tubal spasm rather than true structural occlusion. Fallopian tube recanalization (FTR) is a minimally invasive outpatient procedure that restores patency in 70 to 100% of proximal occlusion cases in pooled literature, with intrauterine pregnancy in 20 to 60% of patients (Roberts, 2023). Mid-segment blockage from prior ligation or ectopic pregnancy may be addressed with microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis. Distal blockage and hydrosalpinx can be treated with neosalpingostomy or fimbrioplasty. Online sources sometimes promote castor oil packs, herbal teas, or fertility cleanses as "natural" ways to unblock fallopian tubes; there is no clinical evidence these address true tubal blockage. The restorative procedures described above are evidence-based, minimally invasive, and treat the actual anatomy. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**2. What is selective HSG?**

In restorative reproductive medicine, [selective salpingography](/glossary/selective-salpingography/) is the diagnostic-and-therapeutic procedure used when a standard HSG shows tubal blockage. Where a diagnostic HSG introduces contrast broadly through the uterus, selective salpingography positions a catheter directly at the opening of the blocked tube to apply focused pressure and confirm whether the obstruction is structural or functional. It can both diagnose and treat proximal blockage in the same sitting by clearing debris or a mucus plug. Major professional society guidelines recommend selective salpingography before more invasive or costly approaches for proximal tubal occlusion. The procedure is outpatient, requires no general anesthesia, and is performed by a clinician with interventional training. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**3. How successful is fallopian tube recanalization?**

Fallopian tube recanalization, often used in restorative reproductive medicine as the first-line option for proximal blockage, achieves technical patency in 70 to 100% of cases in pooled literature (Roberts, 2023). Intrauterine pregnancy rates following FTR range from 20 to 60%, with an average around 30% across studies. Outcomes vary based on the underlying cause of blockage, the presence of other conditions such as endometriosis or chronic endometritis, age, and ovarian reserve. Women with secondary infertility and shorter duration of infertility tend to have better results. These are rates of natural conception following restoration of the tube. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**4. Are blocked tubes a sign I need IVF?**

In restorative reproductive medicine, a blocked-tube finding on HSG is treated as a starting point for investigation, not an automatic indicator for IVF. Blocked fallopian tubes are often treatable, and the appropriate procedure depends on where the blockage sits in the tube and what is causing it. Functional blockages (mucus plugs, transient tubal spasm) frequently clear on their own or with a selective HSG. Structural blockages respond to restorative procedures: fallopian tube recanalization for proximal blockage, microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis for mid-segment damage, and neosalpingostomy or fimbrioplasty for distal blockage. These approaches are evidence-based, minimally invasive, and far less expensive than IVF. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**5. Who treats blocked tubes with restorative procedures?**

[RRM clinicians](/what-is-rrm/) and restorative reproductive surgeons treat blocked fallopian tubes through procedures like selective salpingography, microsurgical anastomosis, and neosalpingostomy. [NaProTechnology](/naprotechnology/)-trained physicians are trained in the surgical and diagnostic approach to tubal factor infertility within a root-cause framework. This includes surgical evaluation and intervention, cycle-based hormonal assessment, and treatment of underlying conditions including endometriosis, [pelvic adhesions](/glossary/pelvic-adhesions/), and chronic infection, all of which can contribute to tubal blockage. Interventional radiologists with fertility training also perform fluoroscopic FTR. When seeking care, ask specifically whether a clinician performs selective salpingography, tubo-tubal anastomosis, or neosalpingostomy. Not every practice offers these procedures, and a direct question about procedure availability will clarify what is offered before committing to a care pathway. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**6. Can I get pregnant after a tubal ligation without IVF?**

In restorative reproductive medicine, pregnancy after tubal ligation is often achievable through microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis. The procedure reconnects the cut ends of the tube under microscopic magnification, restoring natural fertility for many couples. The procedure is restorative: the tube itself is rebuilt rather than bypassed. Outcomes depend on the length and quality of remaining tube, age, and the male partner's semen analysis. RRM clinicians and reproductive surgeons evaluate candidacy before scheduling surgery. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**7. What is hydrosalpinx and can it be treated without IVF?**

Hydrosalpinx is a fluid-filled, sealed fallopian tube, typically caused by past pelvic infection. RRM clinicians often treat it surgically through neosalpingostomy (opening the sealed end) and fimbrioplasty (reconstructing the fimbriae) rather than bypassing it. Both are laparoscopic outpatient procedures. Outcomes depend on the extent of tubal dilation, mucosal preservation, and any concurrent pelvic disease such as endometriosis. A fluid-filled tube left in place can also impair uterine receptivity, which is an argument for treating the tube directly rather than leaving it in place. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**8. What is the best treatment for blocked fallopian tubes?**

In restorative reproductive medicine, the approach to blocked fallopian tubes depends on where the blockage is located: fallopian tube recanalization (FTR) for proximal occlusion, microsurgical tubo-tubal anastomosis for mid-segment damage, and neosalpingostomy or fimbrioplasty for distal blockage or hydrosalpinx. FTR is an outpatient procedure with technical success of 70 to 100% in pooled literature (Roberts, 2023). The clinician must also identify and treat any underlying cause, including endometriosis, chronic endometritis, or pelvic adhesions. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.

**9. Can you get pregnant with blocked fallopian tubes?**

Whether pregnancy is possible with blocked fallopian tubes depends on the location and cause of the blockage and on whether one or both tubes are affected. A unilateral blockage with a patent contralateral tube still allows natural conception, though it may take longer. Bilateral blockage prevents natural conception until the underlying cause is identified and treated. Restorative reproductive medicine offers procedures such as fallopian tube recanalization, microsurgical anastomosis, and neosalpingostomy that aim to restore patency and natural fertility. Pregnancy rates after restoration vary from 20 to 60% depending on the procedure, location of blockage, and concurrent conditions. Consult an RRM clinician or healthcare provider for guidance specific to your situation.
