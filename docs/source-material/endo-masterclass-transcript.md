## Part 1: Pre-Surgery & Decision Making

### Candidates for Surgery
*   Failed medical management (e.g., dysmenorrhea or abnormal uterine bleeding - AUB)
*   Interference with quality of life (e.g., missing excessive work/school)
*   Infertility, recurrent pregnancy loss
*   Symptoms like emesis (vomiting), dyschezia (painful bowel movements), dyspareunia (painful sex)
    *   *Especially associated with menses*
*   History of surgically confirmed endometriosis
    *   *Especially if op report suggests ablation or poor surgical technique*
*   Sonographic evidence of endometriosis (e.g., endometriomas)
*   Consideration of history in the decision-making process:
    *   Strong family history (sisters, mother, etc.)
    *   Personal history of mullerian duct anomalies

### Prepping for Possible Surgery
*   **Imaging:** What should be done?
*   **Gastroenterology:** Often prior diagnosis of IBS, +/- colonoscopy
*   Less classic endometriosis symptoms: bladder pain, back pain
*   Role of physical therapy (before or after)
*   Supplements, diet, lifestyle
*   Birth control
*   **Fertility Treatments** (when to continue or when to hold):
    *   *"Do you give menopur/HCG injectables prior to surgery? How many cycles of treatment prior to surgery?"*

### Prevalence
> "The prevalence of endometriosis in the female population is estimated to be 6 to 10%, and the frequency increases to 24-35% in females with pain and up to 50-60% in women with infertility."

### Decision For Surgery
> "So often we hear patients talk about their endometriosis having recurrence or 'coming back.' The more I learn about endometriosis the more this word, 'recurrence,' bothers me. It suggests a disease that has gone and come back again. In most cases endometriosis recurrence is actually persistence of endometriosis that was never treated due to incomplete surgery."
> -- Dr. Jeff Arrington, MD, FACOG, ACGE

### Creighton Chart Data (Candidates for Surgery)
*   This is the **FIRST** tool that sets NaPro surgeons apart.
*   In a day and age where there is a race for non-invasive biomarker testing to be on the market, CrMS has had this info for decades and for minimal $.
*   **Creighton Chart Abnormalities / Red Flags for endo:**
    *   TEBB especially > 2 days
    *   Heavy menses
    *   Premenstrual spotting
    *   Low cervical mucus score

### Additional Considerations
*   Symptoms (pain, infertility) do not correlate to disease severity (stage).
*   Shared decision-making process.
*   Discuss risks, benefits, alternatives.
*   Discuss theories of endometriosis/various approaches/controversy (e.g., teens):
    *   Earlier intervention and prevention vs.
    *   Earlier intervention and adhesions vs risk of multiple surgeries
*   **Recurrence concerns:**
    *   Approximately 40-60% recurrence for non-excisional surgery (in as little as 1-2 years) after ablation even WITH medical suppression post-operatively.
    *   **Excision:** 75-85% long-term relief.

### Common Endometriosis Myths
*   Pregnancy cures endometriosis.
*   Birth control prevents the spread of endometriosis or treats endometriosis.
*   A normal ultrasound means someone does not have endometriosis.
*   Hysterectomy cures endometriosis.
*   A woman shouldn't care about her symptoms until she is ready to conceive.

## Part 2: Understanding Enemy #1: Endometriosis

### Various Theories of Endometriosis Origin
1.  **Transplantation**
    *   Vascular
    *   Lymphatic
    *   Retrograde Menstruation (Sampson, 1927)
        *   Mainly found in menstruating women
        *   Regular menstruation increases chances
        *   Anatomical location follows patterns
        *   Baboon Model testing
        *   Association with Mullerian anomalies (Obstructive pattern at earlier age)
2.  **Embryonic**
    *   Genetics / Stem Cell / Embryonic stem cell remnants / Primordial germ cells
    *   Multifactorial
    *   Embryologic transformation ("coelomic metaplasia")
    *   Estrogen-driven stimulation
    *   Immune system/ oxidative stress -> promotes growth
    *   Immune dysfunction (can't eliminate menstrual debris)
    *   Apoptosis dysfunction

### Embryonic Origin (Redwine, Opitz 1882)
*   Presence in men
*   After estrogen treatment for prostate cancer (n<20)
*   Due to shared embryonic development at 6-8 weeks gestation
*   Peritoneal pockets
*   Young presentation & aggressive disease
*   Association with Mullerian anomalies (Another embryonic development issue)

**Why retrograde menstruation doesn't make sense to Redwine:**
Redwine asserts: *A "genetically-based embryonic origin explains 100% of all we know about the disease."*

> "Therapies such as hysterectomy, removal of the ovaries, prolonged ovarian suppression by hormonal medical therapies, are based wholly or in part on the theory of reflux menstruation. The number of patients that have received ineffective treatment based in some way on this theory is huge. This and the continuing lack of easily-obtainable evidence makes Sampson's theory of reflux menstruation the most dangerous theory in the history of medicine. Ever.
> When one considers that reflux menstruation can't explain cerebral endometriosis, endometriosis in the thigh, in males, under fingernails, etc, support for the theory on any level is worrisome... Our textbooks should be filled with easily-obtainable photomicrographs of the steps of attachment, proliferation, and invasion. Since there is no robust photomicrographic evidence of those important steps, cartoons are used as 'evidence' in support of this theory."

### Endometriosis and the Immune System
*   History of stress/chronic stress -> increased association (cause or effect).
*   Stress weakens the immune system.
*   Likely immune system dysfunction increases risk (vs consequence of disease).
*   Autoimmune disease well appreciated to peak at age 40 in women.
*   *Dr. Whittaker's belief:* This is why fertility gets much lower faster in some women near 35 (prolonged years of sensitization -- like a bee sting allergy).
*   At age <25, clinical experience demonstrates it is much easier to overcome advanced disease.

## Part 3: Endometriosis and Fertility

*   30-50% of all women with endometriosis have infertility.
*   25%-50% of women with infertility have endometriosis.

### Cervical Mucus Changes
*   It is well understood within NaPro that we see decreased cervical mucus scores with endometriosis patients.
*   **Study Result (Grande G, et al. 2017):** Six proteins were quantitatively increased in endometriosis, almost all involved in the inflammatory pattern. Nine proteins were quantitatively reduced, including proteins related to local innate immunity and protection against oxidative stress. Fifteen proteins were not detected, including proteins involved in antimicrobial activity and related to seminal plasma liquefaction and male fertility.

### How Conception is Affected
All aspects of conception are potentially affected: Egg, Sperm, Cervical mucus, Endometrium (implantation).
*   **Adhesions:** can block ovulation, conception/fallopian tubes
*   **Hormone issues:** (chicken or egg)
*   **LUFs:** (Luteinized Unruptured Follicle Syndrome) / ovulation defects
*   **Miscarriage:** Increased risk
*   **Endometritis:** Increased risk (Impaired implantation, eg. inflammatory cytokine-mediated impairment of endometrial receptivity)
*   **Inflammation:** Impaired conception due to immune system issues, impaired egg quality, sperm damaged by inflammation.
*   **Receptors:** "Decoy" endometrial progesterone receptors (affects implantation)
*   **Transport:** Impaired utero-tubal transport (risk of ectopic pregnancy increased)

## Part 4: OBGYN Training Breakdown

Where does NaPro fit in?
*   **Reproductive Endocrinology and Infertility (REI):** Medical management/IVF, less surgical focus, no OB >12 weeks, no gyn, minimal endometriosis training, rely on IVF, refer some cases to MIGS, minimal to no tubal surgery training (Salpingectomy then IVF).
*   **Minimally Invasive Gyn Surgery (MIGS):** Extensive surgical training on complex disease, hysterectomy, often no OB, extensive endometriosis experience, inconsistent adhesion prevention measures, anti-closure of peritoneum, many not trained on tubal surgery, deep dissection common.
*   **NaPro (Somewhere in between MIGS, REI, and Onc):** Expert on NATURAL fertility (working cooperatively with nature vs. overriding system), meticulous focus on fertility preservation/enhancement, ability to correct tubal disease (instead of salpingectomy > IVF), offers ovarian wedge resections, treats endometriosis like cancer, more consistent anti-adhesion techniques, often takes care of obstetric patients through delivery, avoids extensive dissection when possible, uses PERITONEAL CLOSURE.

## Part 5: Surgery & Techniques

### To Close or Not to Close: Peritoneum Controversy
Data to support peritoneal closure is limited, however it is difficult to study. Urology literature shows some supportive data, while Obstetrics literature leans against it (not strong data). Animal models lean against it.

| Pros of Peritoneal Closure | Cons of Peritoneal Closure |
| :--- | :--- |
| 1. Reapproximate tissues to normal anatomy | 1. No data proves that not closing causes harm |
| 2. Create barrier to reduce infection | 2. Peritoneum heals quickly |
| 3. Minimize wound dehiscence (abdominal wall) | 3. Foreign body and additional manipulation of tissue may worsen adhesions |
| 4. Minimize adhesions | 4. Increased operating time, cost, and anesthesia |

### Diagnostic Laparoscopy
*   Close look / near contact approach.
*   Map of entire abdomen (Systematic, thorough, routine. Evaluates all tissues including diaphragm, gallbladder).
*   Extensive images and/or video documentation.
*   Training to be able to identify all appearances of endometriosis.
*   **Various appearances:** Black ("classic"), Halo, Peritoneal windows/ retraction pockets ("Allen Masters Windows"), Hemosiderin, White scarred areas, Red/clear polyps or vesicles. *(ANYTHING ABNORMAL SHOULD RAISE SUSPICION)*.

### Tools and Techniques: CO2 Laser
*   Minimal lateral thermal spread (10x less thermal spread than incisions made with monopolar electrosurgery).
*   Multi-functional Divergent Beam Technology: Cut (closest) vs. vaporize vs. coagulate (furthest from tissue).
*   More precise layer-by-layer dissection leaves underlying healthy tissue undamaged.
*   Gentle on ovarian tissue, fallopian tubes, etc. (Fewer complications, less concern of damaging ovarian reserve).

### Surgical Technique (Part of Adhesion Prevention)
*   Excision (Including pulling peritoneal pockets inside out)
*   Gentle tissue handling
*   Avoid deep dissection, when possible
*   Avoid thermal damage to healthy tissue
*   **Suture:**
    *   *Selection:* Least inflammatory (Prolene, monocryl, PDS [most favored] > vicryl > chromic [worst])
    *   *Technique:* Running, imbricating closures
*   **Irrigation:** LR or Adept. Frequent use. Clear fluid at end.

### Complex Disease
For complex cases, a second surgery may be needed to:
*   Use the benefits of robotic assistance (Robotic time/team is valuable & often access is difficult).
*   Coordinate with specialists (e.g., colorectal surgery, CT, urology, or general surgery).
*   Counsel on length of surgery and risks for her individual case (e.g., she can decline bowel resection, discuss adhesion prevention techniques like Gortex and a third surgery).

## Part 6: Adhesion Prevention & "Pelvioplasty"

*Pelvioplasty: Plastic surgery of the pelvis.*

**Tools:** CO2 laser, Robot
**Techniques:** Surgical techniques (excise only disease, avoid deep dissection, avoid thermal energy), Clear fluid/hemostasis, Peritoneal closure with running imbricating suture.
**Materials:** Non-inflammatory suture, Slurry (300 cc LR vs adept, 300 mg solucortef, SEPRAFILM), Gortex membrane.

**Literature Review: Cochrane Review (April 2015)**
*Barrier agents for adhesion prevention after gynaecological surgery*
*   Determined by second-look laparoscopy (Intercede, Gortex, Seprafilm, Fibrin sheet).
*   **Conclusions:** No adverse events due to adhesion agents. Low-quality evidence for all (except fibrin) may be more effective than no treatment. No conclusive evidence on relative effectiveness. Quality of evidence: low to moderate.

## Part 8: Applied Knowledge Workbook
### Insider's Guide to Choosing an Endometriosis Surgeon

*More and more surgeons refer to themselves as "excision" or endometriosis surgeons, but their approaches and techniques can be very different. How do you know if you found the one right for you?*

*   *Is he or she receptive to discussing questions?*
*   *Is he or she confident in the answers?*
*   *Do you feel understood, validated, and listened to?*

### Contrast of Endometriosis Surgery Approaches

| Category | BASIC Management / Surgeon | ADVANCED Management / Surgeon |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Non-Surgical Management** | Birth control to "suppress" endometriosis. More likely to say birth control, lupron, hysterectomy or ovary removal are the only long term solutions for pain. | Birth control can suppress symptoms and may alter appearance of implants at surgery. More likely to refer to pelvic floor PT, offer injections or non-narcotic pain options. |
| **Theory** | Endometriosis is a gyn only disease. Retrograde menstruation origin theory. | Endometriosis needs a multidisciplinary approach and is a whole body disease. Embryonic origin of endometriosis. |
| **Diagnosis During Surgery** | Quick look laparoscopy. Regular laparoscopy without near contact technique. Does not look at appendix or diaphragm. | Systematically maps/inspects the pelvis, abdomen, diaphragm. Close look or near contact laparoscopy. Inspects appendix and offers possible appendectomy. Treats diaphragmatic endo. |
| **Surgical Treatment** | Minimal to no pictures. Looks for any obvious dark lesions. May not recognize Allen-Masters windows. Minimal biopsies. Not likely to use adhesion prevention. More likely to drain endometrioma or remove entire ovary. Quicker surgery time. | Multiple pages of pictures or video. Looks for all appearances (clear blebs, halos, scaring, windows). Identifies and excises Allen-Masters windows. Many biopsies sent to path. Removes entire cyst wall of endometrioma. Meticulous, sometimes hours long. |
| **Surgical Volume** | May see 1-2 endometriosis cases per month. | Typically >1-2 endometriosis cases per week. |
| **Hysterectomy Recommendations** | May offer hysterectomy for disease outside of the uterus. May suggest hysterectomy or menopause as a cure. More likely to refer out Stage IV disease. | May offer hysterectomy if adenomyosis is suspected. Emphasizes need for excision of disease outside of the uterus. Often treat stage IV disease with uterine preservation option. |

### Questions to Ask Your Potential Endometriosis Surgeon:
1.  Where do you think endometriosis comes from? What does endometriosis look like?
2.  Do you do diagnostic laparoscopy? If so, when? What techniques do you use?
3.  Do you take pictures or video? Do I get a copy? When or how do I get a summary of the findings? Will I get a copy of the pathology report?
4.  Do you look at the appendix or diaphragm? Do you ever do appendectomies or treat diaphragmatic endometriosis?
5.  When do you recommend birth control or lupron? What does it do for endometriosis? What is the data to support that?
6.  What is the cure for endometriosis?
7.  When do you do a hysterectomy?
8.  On average, how long are your surgeries? How many endometriosis cases do you do per week or month? Do you ever work with subspecialists? How often do you treat stage IV endometriosis?
9.  Did you see any signs of endometrioma on my imaging? How do you treat endometriomas?
10. Do you use adhesion prevention? If so, what?
11. What are additional therapies post-operatively to help manage pelvic pain or painful intercourse?

## Part 9: Surgery Prep, Recovery, and Long-Term Management

### Surgery Prep Basics
*   Financials: Is the surgery covered? Affordable?
*   Avoid fertility-focused intercourse the cycle of surgery.
*   Ask about medications (NSAIDs, Naltrexone).
*   Prep recovery toolkit.
*   Plan ride/hotel/travel.
*   Prep support person.
*   Remove piercings/metal.
*   Prayer card, rosary, scapular or preferred religious material.

### Surgery Recovery Basics (Recovery Toolkit)
*   Tylenol & Motrin
*   Meclizine ("Antivert")
*   Simethicone ("Mylicon")
*   Gum
*   Hot peppermint tea, coffee
*   Heating pad, pillow, stretchy pants
*   Bone broth / pre-made meals
*   Water bottle

### Long Term Management: Lifestyle & Diet
**Lifestyle Basics:**
*   Minimize stress
*   Maximize healthy amounts of sleep
*   Stay hydrated
*   Exercise
*   Epsom baths or magnesium lotions
*   Mental Health
*   Organic tampons or tampon alternatives

**Diet (Anti-inflammatory):**
*   *The ONLY link of increased endometriosis risk includes alcohol and trans fats (Increase estrogen).*
*   Elimination vs. EverlyWell Food Sensitivity test (Common sensitivities: gluten).
*   Whole diet, e.g., Mediterranean.
*   Avoid processed foods.
*   *CAVEAT: DO NOT LET DIET BE A SOURCE OF STRESS.* (Difficult to keep diet consistent; each woman will have a different response).

### Medications & Supplements
**Over the Counter:**
*   **NSAIDs:** Naproxen, Ibuprofen. *(Key: start 1-3 days prior to onset of menses. DO NOT TAKE ON AN EMPTY STOMACH).*
*   **Non-NSAIDs:** Tylenol, Midol (Tylenol + Caffeine + Pyrilamine), Meclizine.

**Data Supported Supplements:**
*   N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC): 1800 mg divided 3 x/day
*   Pycnogenol: 60-100 mg
*   Magnesium: 500 mg/day
*   Ginger: 200 mg every 6 hours
*   Iron: Iron deficiency is common in women with heavy periods. (Use liver capsules or iron + Vitamin C every other day for max absorption).
*   *Other Considerations:* Vitamin D/C/E/B, Zinc, Selenium, Omega 3, Propolis, Quercetin, Curcumin, Probiotics, Resveratrol, Alpha Lipoic Acid, Epigallocatechin-3-gallate.

**Prescriptions & Referrals:**
*   Ponstel
*   Cymbalta (SR) or Amitriptyline
*   Gabapentin
*   Low dose naltrexone (LDN)
*   Progesterone
*   Tranexamic Acid
*   Pelvic Floor Physical Therapy

**Devices:**
*   TENs Unit
*   Castor Oil Pack (Especially for those with constipation)
*   Heating pad
*   Menstrual cup and/or menstrual underwear
