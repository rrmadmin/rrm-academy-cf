## Abstract

No standard criteria exist for how to perform a diagnostic laparoscopy in gynecology. This technical report introduces and describes Systematic Mapping of the Abdomen and Pelvis (S-MAP), a standardized, reproducible protocol for systematic laparoscopic examination of all abdominal and pelvic structures, used in combination with Near-Contact Laparoscopy (NCL), a technique pioneered by Dr. David Redwine [1]. The S-MAP evaluation sequence was first described by Dr. Thomas W. Hilgers in *The Medical and Surgical Practice of NaProTechnology* [2]; the term S-MAP was coined by this author. In a single-surgeon case series over three years using NCL and S-MAP, four rare cancers were incidentally diagnosed and curatively treated during fertility surgeries: three well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumors (NET) of the appendix and one 2 mm well-differentiated papillary mesothelioma (WDPM) of the peritoneum [3]. All four patients were cured. Three of three patients presenting with infertility subsequently achieved pregnancy. The statistical probability of three appendiceal NETs by a single surgeon, given an incidence of 0.95 per 100,000, is approximately 1 x 10^-10 [3]. This case series, combined with previously documented false-negative rates after standard laparoscopy [2], argues for adoption of NCL and S-MAP as the minimum acceptable standard for diagnostic laparoscopy in gynecologic fertility surgery. Standardization of this protocol would enable reproducibility, improved staging, multi-center data collection, and training accountability.

**Keywords:** diagnostic laparoscopy, near-contact laparoscopy, systematic mapping, endometriosis, neuroendocrine tumor, standardization, fertility surgery

## 1. Introduction

Diagnostic laparoscopy is the reference standard for diagnosing pelvic pathology in gynecology, including endometriosis, adhesive disease, and tubal factor infertility. Yet no uniform criteria define what constitutes an adequate diagnostic laparoscopy. The procedure may be performed as a rapid survey of the pelvis under brief anesthesia, or as a meticulous, station-by-station examination of all abdominal and pelvic structures. The clinical consequences of these two approaches are not equivalent.

Women are regularly told their laparoscopy was normal. In a cohort of 46 patients who had been given that designation, repeat examination using a near-contact technique identified pathology in 93.5% [2]. Endometriosis was found in 89.1% of those previously characterized as normal. Only 6.5% had a truly normal pelvis on re-examination [2]. These are not diagnostic discrepancies attributable to interval disease progression. They reflect what was present at the time of the first examination and what the first examination failed to detect.

The problem is technique, not pathology.

Endometriosis presents in multiple visual morphologies: black peritoneal implants, hemosiderin-stained deposits, white scarring, clear or red vesicles, retraction pockets, and Allen-Masters windows. Subtle lesions as small as 2 mm are identifiable under magnified, near-contact visualization [2,3]. At standard viewing distance, these lesions are invisible. A surgeon who does not examine the upper abdomen, the appendix, the terminal ileum, and the diaphragm does not find disease in those locations. Absence of documented examination is not evidence of absence of disease.

This technical report describes two complementary techniques, NCL and S-MAP, that together constitute a reproducible, low-cost protocol overlay on standard laparoscopic practice. We present the operational definitions, equipment, station sequence, biopsy and documentation standards, and a four-case series demonstrating their clinical consequence. The series includes three appendiceal neuroendocrine tumors and one peritoneal mesothelioma diagnosed incidentally during fertility surgeries, all curatively treated, with all three infertile patients subsequently achieving pregnancy.

The argument is not that advanced surgical technique produces better surgical outcomes. The argument is that an unstandardized examination produces an unacceptable false-negative rate, with consequences that extend beyond endometriosis diagnosis into cancer detection and fertility outcomes.

## 2. Background and Rationale

### 2.1 The Problem with Unstandardized Diagnostic Laparoscopy

No consensus guidelines define the scope or sequence of a diagnostic laparoscopy in gynecology [3]. In standard practice, the procedure is often performed as a brief visual survey: a zoomed-out view of the pelvis, limited photography, biopsies of visually obvious lesions only, and no systematic examination of the upper abdomen, appendix, or diaphragm. This approach may be appropriate for some operative indications but is insufficient as a diagnostic examination for endometriosis, infertility, or pelvic pain.

The practical consequence is illustrated in Table 1, which contrasts basic and advanced laparoscopic approaches across key technique domains [see §7, Table 1]. The basic approach produces a record of what was grossly visible. The advanced approach produces a map of every station examined, with photographic documentation and tissue sampling proportional to what was found.

Without standardization, individual surgeons define their own thresholds. These thresholds are not documented, not reproduced between operators, and not available for audit. Research built on laparoscopic reports generated by unstandardized examinations inherits all of that diagnostic noise.

### 2.2 Evidence That Subtle Peritoneal Disease Is Missed

Hilgers documented the false-negative rate in a cohort of 46 patients previously told their laparoscopy was normal (Table 63-1, Chapter 63) [2]. On repeat laparoscopy using near-contact technique, findings were:

- Endometriosis: 41 of 46 (89.1%)
- Adhesive disease: 2 of 46 (4.4%)
- Normal pelvis: 3 of 46 (6.5%)

In aggregate, 43 of 46 patients (93.5%) had pathology not identified at their original examination [2].

Endometriosis lesions measuring 2 mm are identifiable and excisable under near-contact visualization [2,3]. The visual morphology of endometriosis is not confined to the classic black powder-burn appearance. Surgeons operating at standard distance encounter only the most prominent manifestations. Near-contact examination reveals peritoneal pockets, hemosiderin staining, subtle vesicles, and retraction abnormalities that are invisible at standard viewing distance.

The clinical import extends beyond endometriosis. In our series, a 2 mm WDPM on the right pelvic sidewall and three appendiceal neuroendocrine tumors, each measuring between 0.5 mm and 2.5 cm, were identified only because the examination extended to the right pelvic sidewall, the appendix, and the cecum. None of these structures was in the standard pelvic survey field. All were found by deliberately including them in a standardized examination sequence.

## 3. Definitions

### 3.1 Near-Contact Laparoscopy (NCL)

Near-Contact Laparoscopy (NCL) is a laparoscopic examination technique in which the operating surgeon moves the laparoscope into close proximity with the peritoneal surface, beyond the standard working distance. This magnified, close-up view enables detailed visualization of subtle lesions, peritoneal surface changes, and structural abnormalities that are not resolved at standard viewing distance.

The technique provides:

- Magnified visualization of the peritoneal surface
- Improved detection of small and subtle endometriosis lesions, including occult or microscopic lesions
- The close-proximity field necessary to identify and biopsy lesions as small as 2 mm [2,3]

NCL is not a separate surgical procedure. It is a technique applied within any standard laparoscopic case. No additional instruments or equipment beyond those in a standard gynecologic laparoscopy setup are required.

### 3.2 Systematic Mapping of the Abdomen and Pelvis (S-MAP)

S-MAP is a standardized, reproducible protocol for the systematic examination of all abdominal and pelvic structures during diagnostic laparoscopy. Its defining property is a fixed examination sequence: the same anatomic stations are examined in the same order, every case, regardless of presenting indication or prior imaging findings.

S-MAP covers three anatomic regions:

**Upper abdomen:** right and left hemidiaphragm, falciform ligament, gallbladder, liver edge, ligament of Treitz.

**Lower abdomen:** right paracolic gutter, cecum, appendix, terminal ileum, transverse colon, omentum.

**Pelvis:** anterior cul-de-sac, bladder peritoneum, round ligaments, broad ligaments, bilateral ovaries and ovarian fossae, fallopian tubes (proximal, mid, distal with fimbrial evaluation), uterosacral ligaments, posterior cul-de-sac, rectovaginal septum, sigmoid and rectal serosa, pararectal spaces, pelvic sidewalls, Allen-Masters windows (where present).

The core operational properties of S-MAP are:

1. **Systematic and reproducible.** All stations are examined in a consistent sequence. This enables staging documentation, cross-case comparison, and training accountability.
2. **Routine.** The sequence runs every case. Pre-operative imaging does not shorten the examination.
3. **Thorough.** The protocol is designed to identify pathology before the surgeon decides it is not present.

### 3.3 Relationship Between NCL and S-MAP

NCL and S-MAP are complementary, not interchangeable. S-MAP defines *where* to look: the coverage sequence and the anatomic stations that must be documented. NCL defines *how closely* to look at each station.

The complete protocol is: apply NCL at every station of S-MAP, in every case. Neither technique alone reproduces the other's contribution. An examination that is thorough in coverage but performed at standard viewing distance will miss subtle peritoneal lesions. An examination that uses near-contact technique in the pelvis alone will miss disease in the upper abdomen, appendix, and terminal ileum.

### 4.1 Setup and Instruments

S-MAP does not require instrument sets beyond those used in standard gynecologic laparoscopy. The following are employed in our practice:

- High-definition 0-degree and 30-degree laparoscopes
- Uterine manipulator with full range of motion (required for access to the posterior cul-de-sac, posterior uterine serosa, and bilateral ovarian fossae)
- Suprapubic 5 mm trocar with probe and combined irrigation-suction capability
- Atraumatic graspers for tissue mobilization
- Tissue-sparing energy for excision (CO2 laser in our series) [3]
- Adhesion prevention materials staged and available from the outset of the case

Video documentation capability at every station is required under the advanced protocol standard. Still photography of individual lesions supplements the video record.

When excisional or reconstructive surgery is anticipated or discovered intraoperatively, adhesion prevention barriers (Gore-Tex membrane and/or Seprafilm-steroid-lactated Ringer solution slurry) are prepared before the case begins. ### 4.2 Standardized Station Sequence

The examination follows a fixed directional path: a single continuous loop that starts at the uterine fundus, sweeps the upper abdomen, descends through the lateral and posterior compartments, and ends at the posterior cul-de-sac (Figure 1). The route is performed in the same direction in every case. Altering the sequence for convenience or time pressure is inconsistent with the protocol and defeats the reproducibility benefit.

[Figure 1: S-MAP route diagram. Single continuous sweep from uterine fundus (Start) through the upper abdomen, lateral gutters, and posterior compartment to the posterior cul-de-sac (End). Adapted from Hilgers TW, Figure 63-13, *Medical & Surgical Practice of NaProTechnology*, Pope Paul VI Institute Press, Omaha NE, 2004.]

The 12 stations below are the textual decomposition of this route. Each station names a discrete anatomic region examined under NCL along the path.

**Station 1: Umbilical entry and far-view orientation.**
Following trocar placement, a panoramic view of the abdominal and pelvic cavity is obtained. General orientation, free fluid assessment, and bowel positioning are documented before any close-up examination.

**Station 2: Right hemidiaphragm and upper right quadrant.**
The scope is directed cephalad. The right hemidiaphragm is examined at near-contact range. The falciform ligament, liver edge, and gallbladder are inspected. Diaphragmatic implants may be visible as dark discolorations, retraction defects, or erythematous peritoneal changes.

**Station 3: Left hemidiaphragm and upper left quadrant.**
The left hemidiaphragm is examined. Symmetric examination of both hemidiaphragms is required; disease distribution is not predictable from symptom laterality.

**Station 4: Right paracolic gutter, cecum, and appendix.**
The scope advances to the right lower quadrant. The cecum is examined. The appendix is identified and inspected along its entire length using NCL. Appendiceal abnormalities include surface discoloration, induration, mass effect, adhesions, and endometriotic implants. Hemosiderin staining of the appendiceal surface, even without mass, warrants biopsy. An appendiceal mass or significant inflammatory change meets the threshold for appendectomy, performed at the index operation.

**Station 5: Terminal ileum, omentum, and transverse colon.**
The terminal ileum is examined for serosal implants. Implants on the terminal ileum may be subtle and are missed without systematic inspection of this structure.

**Station 6: Pelvic entry and anterior pelvis.**
The anterior cul-de-sac, bladder peritoneum, round ligaments, and anterior broad ligaments are examined at NCL distance. Bladder peritoneum involvement is a common site of endometriotic disease and may present as peritoneal thickening, vascularization, or subtle surface changes without overt black implants.

**Station 7: Bilateral ovaries and ovarian fossae.**
Each ovary is examined with the grasper to mobilize it from the pelvic sidewall and permit examination of the ovarian fossa floor. Endometriomas are identified and managed per operative plan. The ovarian surface, periovarian adhesions, and the peritoneum of the ovarian fossa are all examined at near-contact range.

**Station 8: Fallopian tubes (bilateral).**
Proximal, mid-segment, and distal tube with fimbria are examined. Peritubal adhesions, endometriotic deposits on the tubal serosa (proximal and distal), and fimbrial architecture are documented. Tubal patency is assessed by transcervical indigo carmine dye injection.

**Station 9: Uterosacral ligaments, posterior cul-de-sac, and rectovaginal septum.**
The uterosacral ligaments are examined bilaterally using NCL. Deep infiltrating disease, peritoneal pockets, and Allen-Masters windows are located in this region. The posterior cul-de-sac and rectovaginal septum are examined by gentle mobilization of the uterus and manipulation of the posterior compartment. In the presence of frozen pelvis, the posterior cul-de-sac may be inaccessible; this is documented as such, not left unrecorded.

**Station 10: Sigmoid, rectal serosa, and pararectal spaces.**
Sigmoid serosal involvement and pararectal endometriosis are examined. Deeply infiltrating disease here may require colorectal surgical consultation, staged at the index operation or as a planned second procedure.

**Station 11: Pelvic sidewalls (bilateral).**
Both pelvic sidewalls are examined. The right pelvic sidewall is the site of the WDPM identified in Case A [3]. Sidewall disease is not evident on standard survey.

**Station 12: Documentation loop.**
Before closure, a final video and still-image capture pass is performed at each station documented as abnormal. This creates the photographic record for pathology correlation, staging, and the surgical report.

### 4.3 Biopsy, Excision, and Sampling Standards

The threshold for biopsy is low. Any peritoneal surface abnormality warrants tissue sampling. This includes subtle color change, thickening, retraction, vascular engorgement, or surface irregularity. Surgeons practicing standard laparoscopy biopsy visually obvious lesions. S-MAP/NCL practice biopsies anything that does not look normal under near-contact examination.

Excision is preferred over ablation for endometriotic lesions in the context of restorative reproductive intent. Excision provides a tissue specimen for histologic confirmation, removes the full depth of disease rather than the surface only, and is the standard consistent with RRM surgical principles.

Appendix examination is mandatory at every S-MAP case. Appendectomy is offered when inspection reveals mass, induration, hemosiderin staining, or any abnormality on the appendiceal surface. In Cases B, C, and D of this series, the appendiceal NET would have remained undetected without this step [3].

Adhesion prevention is integrated into the index case, not deferred. Gore-Tex membrane and Seprafilm-steroid-lactated Ringer slurry were used in our series [3].

### 4.4 Documentation Standard

S-MAP produces a standardized surgical record. Each station examined generates a photographic or video entry in the operative documentation. Pathology specimens are labeled with their station of origin so that pathologic findings can be traced to an anatomic location in the map.

This documentation standard is the foundation for research utility. A cohort of S-MAP cases produces a station-resolved data set. A cohort of standard-lap cases produces a record of what was grossly abnormal. These are not equivalent research substrates.

## 5. Safety, Risk, and Informed Consent

S-MAP extends operating time compared to a brief pelvic survey. The additional time reflects thorough execution of the full station sequence. Extended Trendelenburg positioning carries cumulative venous thromboembolism risk, which is communicated as part of pre-operative informed consent.

Entry-technique safety is established in the laparoscopic literature. A Cochrane review encompassing 57 studies and 9,865 participants found no mortality attributable to laparoscopic entry technique across methods [5]. S-MAP does not alter the entry technique. The extended operating time is introduced by the examination sequence, not by the entry.

Pre-operative patient counseling for S-MAP cases should address:

- Expected operating time reflecting systematic examination rather than a quick survey
- The possibility that disease found intraoperatively may require a staged second procedure (e.g., colorectal or cardiothoracic consultation for disease beyond gynecologic scope)
- The possibility that incidental non-gynecologic pathology may be identified

This third point is not standard in gynecologic pre-operative counseling. Our series argues that it should be. Three of four patients had appendiceal pathology; one had a peritoneal lesion pathologically distinct from endometriosis. Neither finding was suspected pre-operatively.

When advanced disease is identified that exceeds the surgeon's scope, the appropriate response is to halt, document the finding, and arrange subspecialty consultation. Colorectal surgeons (for bowel disease), cardiothoracic surgeons (for diaphragmatic disease), and urologists (for ureteral involvement) may be added to a staged or same-session case depending on the finding and institutional logistics [3].

## 6. Results: Case Series

### 6.1 Single-Surgeon Detection Record

Over three years of practice using NCL and S-MAP exclusively as the diagnostic laparoscopy standard, a single non-oncologic gynecologic surgeon with focused Practice Designation in Minimally Invasive Gynecologic Surgery (MIGS) by ABOG diagnosed and curatively treated four rare cancers as incidental findings during surgeries performed for pain or infertility [3].

- Three cases: well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumor (NET), WHO grade 1, of the appendix
- One case: well-differentiated papillary mesothelioma (WDPM) of the right peritoneum, 2 mm
- All four patients: cured
- Three of three infertile patients in the series: subsequent pregnancy achieved [3]

The incidence of appendiceal neuroendocrine neoplasia is 0.95 per 100,000 [6]. The probability of a single surgeon encountering three such cases by chance is approximately 1 x 10^-10 [3]. No other malignancies were encountered in the series.

### 6.2 Case Vignettes

**Case A: Well-Differentiated Papillary Mesothelioma of the Right Peritoneum**

A 35-year-old G0 presented with dysmenorrhea, abnormal uterine bleeding, infertility, dyspareunia, incomplete bowel and bladder emptying, and a known fibroid uterus with the largest leiomyoma measuring 7.3 cm. She had no known history of asbestos exposure.

She underwent hysteroscopy, selective hysterosalpingogram with bilateral tubal cannulation, and diagnostic laparoscopy using NCL and S-MAP. Intraoperative findings included bilateral obstructed fallopian tubes with normal intratubal pressures after endosalpingolysis, multiple endometrial and endocervical polyps, and external compression of the right fallopian tube by the adjacent leiomyomas.

Subsequent surgery included robotic-assisted myomectomy of seven leiomyomas with bagged morcellation, laser excision of visually apparent endometriosis, removal of a left paratubal cyst, and excision of a right ovarian lesion.

Pathology was negative for endometriosis. The right pelvic sidewall specimen, a 0.2 cm lesion identified only under near-contact visualization, returned positive for well-differentiated papillary mesothelioma. The right ovarian lesion was a serous papillary fibroma, an incidental benign finding. Gynecologic oncology and general oncology were consulted. Recurrence risk was characterized as low. CT surveillance every 3 to 6 months for five years was recommended. All pain, bowel, and urinary symptoms resolved postoperatively.

WDPM is an extremely rare peritoneal neoplasm, distinct from malignant mesothelioma both clinically and genetically [7,8]. Approximately 50 case reports appear in the literature. The lesion typically presents in reproductive-age women and is usually an incidental surgical finding; some patients present with abdominal pain, ascites, or abnormal uterine bleeding [7,8]. Asbestos exposure is present in only a subset of cases. The 2 mm dimension in Case A is below the threshold of detection at standard laparoscopic viewing distance. Without NCL applied to the right pelvic sidewall during S-MAP, this lesion would not have been identified or resected.

**Case B: Appendiceal Neuroendocrine Tumor, WHO Grade 1, 0.5 mm**

A 27-year-old G0 presented as a second opinion for ongoing severe dysmenorrhea after prior surgery for endometriosis at an academic center, which had included treatment of a 7 cm left ovarian endometrioma. A 1 cm endometrioma was present on current ultrasound.

She underwent hysteroscopy, endometrial and endocervical cultures and biopsies, selective hysterosalpingogram, bilateral tubal cannulation, diagnostic laparoscopy with NCL and S-MAP, and appendectomy.

Intraoperative findings included hysteroscopic evidence of endometritis, bilateral partial tubal occlusion opened to normal pressures after cannulation, adhesions of the left fallopian tube and left ovary to the pelvic sidewall, extensive uterine serosal inflammation and adhesions, endometriosis on both the proximal and distal fallopian tube, stage IV endometriosis, and hemosiderin staining of the appendix.

Appendectomy was performed on the basis of the appendiceal surface findings. Pathology confirmed a well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumor, WHO grade 1, with a maximum microscopic dimension of 0.5 mm, free margins, tumor confined to the submucosa, and two negative lymph nodes. She was evaluated by oncology and determined to be cured of appendiceal cancer.

Following oncologic clearance, she returned for robotic-assisted laser excision of endometriosis, left ovarian cystectomy, lysis of adhesions, and adhesion prevention, with subsequent dramatic improvement of her dysmenorrhea.

**Case C: Stage IV Appendiceal Neuroendocrine Tumor with Pelvic Metastases and PCOS**

A 33-year-old G3P2012 presented with long, irregular cycles, abnormal uterine bleeding, dysmenorrhea, continuous mucus discharge, and infertility. She carried a diagnosis of PCOS, confirmed by elevated 17-hydroxyprogesterone (up to 988 ng/dL), exclusion of non-classical congenital adrenal hyperplasia by endocrinology, LH:FSH reversal greater than 2:1, and elevated testosterone. Non-classical CAH was ruled out. Ultrasound demonstrated bilaterally enlarged polycystic-appearing ovaries.

She underwent hysteroscopy, endometrial and endocervical cultures and biopsies, selective hysterosalpingogram, bilateral tubal cannulation, diagnostic laparoscopy with NCL and S-MAP, robotic-assisted bilateral ovarian wedge resection, laser excision of endometriosis, appendectomy, cauterization of cervical ectropion, and adhesion prevention.

Intraoperative findings included an enlarged uterus suspicious for adenomyosis; endometriosis involving the bladder, uterus, posterior broad ligaments, and posterior cul-de-sac; a left paratubal cyst; a right ovarian cyst; bilaterally enlarged polycystic ovaries; bilaterally partially obstructed fallopian tubes; a mass on the appendix; and a grade III cervical ectropion.

Pathology identified stage IV well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumor, WHO grade 1, of the appendix, with the appendiceal mass measuring 2.5 cm. Two additional pelvic specimens (measuring 0.3 to 0.8 cm per pathology report) were also positive for neuroendocrine tumor, indicating metastatic pelvic disease.

She underwent right hemicolectomy. Twenty-seven lymph nodes, all margins, and pelvic tissue were negative. No chemotherapy or radiation was indicated. Following two years of serial CT surveillance, she was determined to be in remission. During this surveillance period, pregnancy was strongly inadvisable to permit imaging. She used the Creighton Model FertilityCare System with early luteal-phase progesterone monitoring for family planning. This method has demonstrated a conception rate of 0 pregnancies in 605 cycles, equivalent to approximately 50 couple-years of exposure [9].

During the surveillance period, the patient's mother (age 64) was diagnosed with breast cancer. Genetic evaluation identified an ATM gene mutation in both patient and mother, conferring an approximately 25% lifetime risk of breast cancer for the patient. She is enrolled in a breast surveillance program with alternating MRI and mammogram every six months and is planning genetic testing for her children.

This case illustrates the full scope of NCL and S-MAP as a diagnostic platform: a single operative session produced the diagnosis of stage IV appendiceal NET with pelvic metastases, excision of stage IV endometriosis, wedge resection of polycystic ovaries, Creighton Model-guided fertility management, and initiation of a hereditary cancer surveillance cascade.

**Case D: Appendiceal Neuroendocrine Tumor with Frozen Pelvis, Diaphragmatic Disease, and Subsequent Spontaneous Conception Following FSH 34.6**

A 27-year-old G1P1 presented two days after an outside diagnostic laparoscopy performed at another institution while the patient was pursuing fertility treatment. The outside operative report documented bilateral hydrosalpinges, bilateral endometriomas (left endometrioma ruptured intraoperatively with chocolate fluid), obliteration of the posterior cul-de-sac with bowel adherent to the posterior uterus, serosal uterine implants, posterior bladder implants, and cecal adhesions. The appendix could not be confidently identified by the outside surgeon.

The outside surgeon recommended hysterectomy and bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy with general surgery on standby for possible bowel resection. The patient declined in favor of fertility-sparing management and presented for a second opinion.

Her prior workup included three IUIs on letrozole, with worsening pain following each cycle. Semen analysis (5/20) showed: volume 1 mL, concentration 28 million/mL, motility 37%, total motile count 10 million, morphology 91% abnormal. Colonoscopy performed in summer 2021 was normal. Emergency department CT performed for post-operative bilious emesis and 20-pound weight loss showed the appendix "suspicious for appendicitis, periappendiceal inflammatory changes," retrospectively consistent with occult appendiceal NET.

**Surgery 2 (Whittaker, NCL and S-MAP):**

Operative findings included an appendiceal and cecal mass; a completely obstructed right fallopian tube, recovered with tubal cannulation; a uterine septum, resected at the same operation; bilateral endometriomas; frozen pelvis with rectum and bowel adherent to the posterior uterus; extensive endometriosis involving the uterus, bilateral adnexa, bladder, right abdominal sidewall, inferior and lateral to the right hemidiaphragm, the diaphragm near the falciform ligament, and the terminal ileum; and an obliterated posterior cul-de-sac.

Laparoscopy was performed using NCL and S-MAP, permitting identification of the appendiceal mass, the diaphragmatic implants, and the terminal ileum disease that were not visualized at the prior outside laparoscopy.

Appendix and partial cecum were resected. Pathology: well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumor, WHO grade 1, of the appendix, measuring 1.5 cm; uninvolved margins; foci suspicious for lymphovascular invasion (artifact not excluded on processing). Cecal serosa: endometriosis. Endometrial biopsy: inactive endometrium with a few plasma cells on CD138 immunostain; clinical correlation recommended for chronic endometritis.

Oncology consultation presented the options of observation versus right hemicolectomy. Patient chose observation.

**Surgery 3 (Fertility-Preserving Reconstruction):**

Robotic-assisted laser excision of endometriosis; bilateral ovarian cystectomy; right fimbrioplasty; lysis of adhesions; excision of adenomyosis; robotic-assisted laparoscopic ileocecectomy with anastomosis (ileocecal valve nodule: pathology confirmed endometriosis). Adhesion prevention: Gore-Tex membrane implant, Seprafilm, 300 mg solu-cortef (steroid), and 300 mL lactated Ringer solution administered as an intraoperative slurry. Gore-Tex membrane removed on postoperative day 10.

**Surgery 5 (Combined Procedure with Cardiothoracic Surgery):**

Six months after Surgery 3, known deep diaphragmatic endometriosis was addressed in a combined case with cardiothoracic surgery. Findings: extensive diaphragmatic endometriosis, minimal residual pelvic endometriosis (one small endometrioma and one lesion), and extensive pelvic adhesions involving large bowel and bilateral adnexa obliterating the posterior cul-de-sac. Procedure: diaphragm stripping with cardiothoracic surgery; resection of residual endometriosis; right ovarian cystectomy; bilateral ovarian suspension; lysis of adhesions. Intraoperative slurry: Seprafilm, 300 mg solu-cortef, 300 mL lactated Ringer solution.

**Post-operative Reproductive Course:**

Postoperative FSH on cycle day 3 was 34.6 mIU/mL. Prognosis for fertility was characterized as guarded. DHEA supplementation was initiated. Ovulation induction, trigger medication, and timed intercourse were attempted and failed. Cervical mucus, which had been absent preoperatively, improved postoperatively.

The patient conceived spontaneously with luteal-phase support within one year of her last surgery, and nine months after the FSH 34.6 draw. Pregnancy was monitored with serial progesterone measurements referenced to the Pope Paul VI Institute normative curve (N=109).

She delivered a healthy female infant at 36 weeks gestation via cesarean section, precipitated by preterm labor in the context of prior uterine surgery. Postpartum, she experienced severe postpartum depression requiring inpatient psychiatric admission. She had received prophylactic intramuscular progesterone support in the postpartum period; progesterone levels throughout pregnancy had tracked below the mean of the normative reference curve.

### 6.3 Statistical Inference

The incidence of appendiceal neuroendocrine neoplasia in a general population is 0.95 per 100,000 [6]. Three such diagnoses by a single surgeon over three years, in a practice population of reproductive-age women presenting for fertility or pain evaluation, produces a probability of approximately 1 x 10^-10 [3]. This is not a plausible frequency under random chance.

Two interpretations are consistent with this observation. First, surgical technique drives detection: the appendix is examined and biopsied when standard practice would not inspect it, producing diagnoses that would otherwise remain silent. Second, the patient population may carry elevated risk: women with endometriosis or infertility may have a higher prevalence of appendiceal neuroendocrine neoplasia than the general population. The latter hypothesis is currently unsupported by population data; no risk factors for appendiceal NET have been reported in the endometriosis or infertility literature [6].

The practical implication is the same under either interpretation: systematic appendiceal examination during gynecologic laparoscopy identifies a cancer that standard examination misses. The clinical and oncologic consequence of a missed appendiceal NET that reaches a size or stage where it presents symptomatically is substantially worse than an incidentally identified, surgically cured grade 1 lesion.

## 7. Basic Versus Advanced Laparoscopy: A Comparison of Technique Standards

Table 1 contrasts the basic laparoscopic approach, reflecting common practice norms, with the advanced approach using NCL and S-MAP.

| Domain | Basic Approach | Advanced Approach (NCL + S-MAP) |
|---|---|---|
| Examination method | Zoomed-out survey of the pelvis | Systematic, station-by-station mapping with near-contact visualization at each station |
| Appendix | Not routinely examined | Examined at every case; appendectomy offered if indicated |
| Diaphragm | Not examined | Examined bilaterally at each case; treated if disease found |
| Documentation | Limited photographs; obvious lesions only; few biopsies | Video and photographic documentation at each station; biopsies of all abnormal peritoneum |
| Endometrioma management | Drainage or oophorectomy | Cystectomy with ovarian preservation |
| Advanced or stage IV disease | May recommend hysterectomy | Complete excision with fertility preservation where possible |
| Adhesion prevention | Not incorporated | Integrated into the index case |
| Upper abdomen | Not examined | Included in the standard S-MAP sequence |

## 8. Discussion

The central argument of this paper is not that a more careful surgeon finds more disease. The argument is that no standardized minimum exists for what constitutes a diagnostic laparoscopy, and that this gap produces a documented, quantifiable false-negative rate with clinical consequences across multiple domains: delayed endometriosis diagnosis, missed non-gynecologic malignancy, and suboptimal fertility outcomes.

### 8.1 Why Standardization Matters

Standardization produces reproducibility. A surgeon who follows S-MAP leaves a station-resolved operative record. Deviation from the sequence is documentable. Multi-center prospective studies require a shared procedural reference; an unspecified "diagnostic laparoscopy" is not that reference.

Training accountability is a related benefit. A trainee performing their first 50 laparoscopies under an S-MAP protocol acquires a systematic habit. The habit persists; the habit produces the examination; the examination finds the disease. Current residency training in laparoscopy, primarily derived from sterilization procedures, does not develop this skill set [2].

### 8.2 NCL and S-MAP Beyond Endometriosis

The case series demonstrates that NCL and S-MAP function as a general diagnostic platform, not an endometriosis-specific tool. The appendiceal NETs in Cases B, C, and D were not suspected preoperatively. The WDPM in Case A was invisible at standard distance. These diagnoses were incidental to the stated surgical indication in each case. They were made because the examination reached the anatomic site where the pathology resided.

Appendiceal neuroendocrine neoplasia is the most common appendiceal malignancy. Its indolent biology means that symptomatic presentation is a late event. Incidental detection at a stage when the appendix is the only site of disease is curative without chemotherapy or radiation, as demonstrated in Cases B and C. Stage IV disease (Case C) required hemicolectomy but was still cured with surgical management alone.

### 8.3 Boundaries: When to Involve Subspecialists

S-MAP may surface disease beyond the scope of the operating gynecologic surgeon. Diaphragmatic endometriosis requiring stripping, ileocecal valve resection for bowel endometriosis, and appendiceal mass management requiring oncologic decision-making all arose in this series. The appropriate response is intraoperative recognition, documentation, and either concurrent subspecialty involvement (as in Surgery 5, with cardiothoracic surgery) or planned staged management.

The protocol does not require the gynecologic surgeon to operate outside their competency. It requires the surgeon to look everywhere and to recognize what is found.

### 8.4 Limitations

This series represents a single non-oncologic surgeon's three-year experience in a second-opinion practice population. Second-opinion patients self-select for complexity; the prevalence of advanced disease, prior failed treatment, and atypical presentations is higher than in a primary-care gynecology population. The incidental cancer detection rate in this series may not reflect what an S-MAP adopter would encounter in a less selected population, though the false-negative data from Hilgers's repeat-laparoscopy cohort [2] is independent of practice type.

No comparator group exists. A randomized trial comparing S-MAP to standard-technique diagnostic laparoscopy cannot be conducted ethically once the false-negative rate of the standard technique is known. The design constraint is inherent to the question.

WDPM incidence in the general population is unknown. The case-report literature does not support a denominator. The argument that NCL and S-MAP detected a lesion that would otherwise have been missed rests on the 2 mm dimension of the Case A lesion and the documented resolution limit of standard viewing distance, not on a population incidence comparison.

### 8.5 Call to Action

Standardization of diagnostic laparoscopy is prerequisite to advancing endometriosis science. Without a defined protocol, published laparoscopic series are not comparable across institutions. Disease prevalence data derived from standard-technique laparoscopy underestimates true prevalence. Treatment outcome data attached to incomplete staging underestimates true disease burden.

The AAGL and other professional bodies have addressed standardization in operative laparoscopy. The diagnostic examination has received less attention. NCL and S-MAP are low-cost, technically accessible, and documentable. Their adoption requires a decision to standardize, not a new technology.

## 9. Conclusion

Near-Contact Laparoscopy and Systematic Mapping of the Abdomen and Pelvis constitute a paired, reproducible protocol that closes the gap between what diagnostic laparoscopy is typically performed as and what it is capable of as a diagnostic examination. The protocol is overlaid on standard laparoscopic practice with existing instruments and generates a station-resolved operative record.

In this series, applying the protocol produced four incidental cancer diagnoses that standard examination would not have provided, all cured; three fertility outcomes in patients who had sought treatment elsewhere; and a longitudinal case demonstrating that restorative fertility surgery, even following FSH 34.6 and multiple prior failed interventions, can result in spontaneous conception and live birth.

The next steps are adoption and registry. Multi-surgeon implementation of S-MAP, with prospective data collection, would establish the detection rate differential between S-MAP and standard laparoscopy in an unselected surgical population. The data needed to prove the case already exist in the false-negative literature [2]. What the field needs now is a named, citable, adoptable protocol, and the will to require it.
