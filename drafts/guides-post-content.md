## If You Are New to RRM, Read This First

Start with the [RRM Glossary](/glossary/) to learn the vocabulary, then read [What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine?](/what-is-rrm/) for the full framework. After that, pick the guide matching your situation: NaProTechnology, FEMM, or NeoFertility. Total reading time is about 150 minutes.

Restorative Reproductive Medicine is a diagnostic and therapeutic framework. It identifies and treats the underlying causes of reproductive dysfunction rather than bypassing them. Five structured guides cover the full scope of RRM. They are not introductory overviews. Each one is a working map of a specific area. Together they give you enough foundation to ask the right questions, recognize the right care, and move forward.

## The Five Guides and Who Each One Is For

Not every guide is the right starting point for every reader. Here is how to match them to where you are.

The [RRM Glossary](/glossary/) is for the vocabulary-first reader. RRM uses precise clinical terms: luteal phase defect, peak-type mucus, progesterone supplementation, selective salpingography. Before any clinical conversation makes full sense, you need these definitions. The glossary covers 159 terms across eight subject categories. Start here if you feel lost in the language.

[NaProTechnology: A Clinician's Guide](/naprotechnology/) is for the reader who has heard the name NaProTechnology and wants the clinical picture. What does a NaPro-trained clinician actually evaluate? What protocols does the approach use? What conditions does it address? This guide answers those questions directly. NaProTechnology is one application of RRM principles, developed at the Pope Paul VI Institute. It is not a synonym for RRM. Understanding the distinction matters.

[What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine?](/what-is-rrm/) is for the reader who wants the full framework. This is the longest guide. It covers the history of the field, the evidence base, the conditions RRM addresses, and how RRM compares to conventional reproductive medicine. If you want context before choosing a path, read this one.

[What is FEMM?](/femm/) is for the reader interested in a FABM-based approach that centers the medical interpretation of biological cycle markers. FEMM has its own training system and its own clinical applications. This guide maps both.

[What is NeoFertility?](/neofertility/) is for the reader researching Dr. Phil Boyle's model of care. Dr. Boyle's work in Dublin contributed some of the field's most cited outcome data. This guide covers his approach and what distinguishes it within the broader RRM tradition.

### For Patients Newly Referred or Self-Referred

Start with the glossary. Read it lightly, not to memorize, but to orient. Then move to What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine? for the framework. Once you have both, you will understand what questions to bring to a first appointment.

### For Clinicians Evaluating RRM for Their Practice

Read NaProTechnology: A Clinician's Guide first. It gives you the protocol-level picture. Then read What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine? for the evidence base and the broader context. Both together run about 75 minutes.

## How the Guides Fit Together

The guides are not competing documents. They build on each other.

The glossary defines terms used across all four content guides. Read it alongside any of the others when a term stops you. What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine? is the umbrella: it establishes the framework that the other three guides operate within. NaProTechnology, FEMM, and NeoFertility are each specific applications of RRM principles. They are not separate philosophies. They are different expressions of the same cause-based approach, developed in different clinical settings, by different practitioners, over different timelines.

A reader who finishes all five guides will find they cover the same core commitments from different angles: diagnose before treating, treat the body's actual condition, measure cycle function rather than suppress it.

## What a Guide Gives You That an FAQ Cannot

A single FAQ answers a single question. That is useful when you already know what you are asking. Most people starting out do not know what they do not know. A long-form guide builds a mental model. It connects the pieces: why cycle charting matters for diagnosis, why a luteal phase defect is not a minor footnote, why the same underlying hormone pattern can appear in infertility, recurrent loss, and painful periods. That connective tissue is what the guides provide.

Each guide takes between 15 and 60 minutes to read. That range reflects the difference in depth across the five. The glossary can be scanned quickly. What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine? is built for sustained reading. The time investment is the point. You are not looking up a fact. You are building a working knowledge of a clinical paradigm.

## A Reading Path for 150 Minutes

You do not need to read all five guides at once. Read for the question you have right now.

Here is a sequence that works for most readers:

Spend 10 minutes scanning the glossary. Do not read every entry. Look up terms you have already encountered and cannot define precisely. Flag ones you want to return to.

Then read What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine? It runs about 60 minutes. It is the one guide that gives you the full picture: the field's origins, the evidence, the clinical approach, and what distinguishes it from the conventional model.

After that, go to the guide that matches your situation. NaProTechnology if you have been referred to a NaPro clinician or are evaluating the approach. FEMM if you are working with a FEMM instructor or considering that method. NeoFertility if you are researching Dr. Boyle's clinic or outcomes data.

When the guides are absorbed, the [research library](/library/) is the next layer. It holds the peer-reviewed studies behind the clinical claims in every guide. The library is searchable. You can move from a concept in a guide directly to the studies that support it.

The [guides index](/guides/) lists all five in one place if you want to see them together before deciding where to start.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does it take to read all five guides?

About 150 minutes total. Individual guides range from 15 to 60 minutes depending on depth. The glossary is the shortest. What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine? is the longest.

### Do I need to read the guides in order?

No. The glossary and What is Restorative Reproductive Medicine? are the most useful starting points for most readers. The NaProTechnology, FEMM, and NeoFertility guides can be read in any order after that, or on their own if your situation points to one specifically.
